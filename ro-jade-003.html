RO-JADE-003 · Neolithic Ritual Jade

Twin Zoomorphic Figure with Large Ears

Light olive-toned nephrite, Hongshan culture, SEM-documented archaeological patina

Nephrite (Tremolite–Actinolite) Hongshan Culture SEM-Verified Patina Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Optical / SEM Microscopy — Surface Detail

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a twin (paired) zoomorphic carving in light olive-toned nephrite with faint zonal banding, executed in the totemic-sculpture tradition associated with the Hongshan culture. Both halves of the composition depict beings with large, elongated ears — in archaic iconography of this type, prominent ears are generally read as markers of heightened spiritual receptivity and a "listening" function toward the world of spirits and ancestors.

The composition is vertically oriented, with generalised anatomy and massive forms characteristic of late Neolithic ritual jades; the symmetry of the two paired figures underscores a theme of duality and sacred balance. A pair of drilled perforations is present on the reverse side, most plausibly intended for attachment to a ritual mount or for suspension. Carving is entirely by hand, with ancient abrasive traces and soft, uneven polishing consistent with stone-tool working; no indication of modern instrumental tooling is visible.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Scientific Examination — SEM Documentation

According to the accompanying documentation, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was carried out on this object (Institute of Steel and Alloys, Moscow), with the following observations reported:

  • A dense layer of microcrystalline calcite (CaCO₃) with a naturally heterogeneous structure.
  • Secondary inclusions of iron and manganese oxides, consistent with prolonged mineralisation in a burial environment.
  • Micropores with silicate–carbonate infill, consistent with geochemical ageing over an extended period.
  • Surface micro-roughness ("dermatoglyphic"-type texture) not reproducible by modern finishing methods.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, this combination — a dense, unevenly crystallised calcite layer together with secondary iron/manganese inclusions and micropore infill — is consistent with a mineralised secondary surface layer formed over a long burial interval, rather than with a superficially applied coating.

The SEM observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; the underlying SEM imagery itself has not been independently re-examined for this Atlas entry. They are presented as supporting evidence alongside the macroscopic description, not as a standalone, independently re-verified laboratory result of this page.

Section 3

Cultural and Symbolic Context

The twin form is interpreted, per the accompanying documentation, as an expression of Hongshan dualistic cosmology, in which the worlds of the living and of spirits, the masculine and the feminine, the celestial and the terrestrial, are held in a state of mutual balance. Within this interpretive tradition, the large ears are read as:

  • the capacity to hear the voices of ancestors;
  • receptivity to sacred signals;
  • an attribute of shamanic mediation.

On this reading, the figure may have served as a ritual totem or protective amulet associated with ancestor worship.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date or function.

According to the accompanying documentation, the object has been considered by:

  • The Scientific Centre for Certification and Appraisal of Jewellery and Antiques (Moscow, 2012).
  • The Collegium of Experts and Appraisers of Jewellery and Antiques, under the direction of A. G. Gagarin.
  • Referenced in: N.D. Dronova, What You Need to Know About Ancient Chinese Jade, Moscow, 2017. ISBN 978-5-9909531-3-0.

Condition, per the accompanying documentation: the object is in excellent archaeological condition, with no indication of restoration. The surface carries a stable, multi-layered patina; edges show natural smoothing consistent with age; microcracks and cavities consistent with prolonged burial are present.
