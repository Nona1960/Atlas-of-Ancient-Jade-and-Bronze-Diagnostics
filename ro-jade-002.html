RO-JADE-002 · Neolithic Ritual Jade

Amulet in the Form of a Stylised Anthropo-Zoomorphic Being

Grey-green nephrite carving with calcite encrustation, Hongshan culture

Nephrite (Tremolite–Actinolite) Hongshan Culture Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) Calcite Encrustation

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a small vertical carving in grey-green nephrite, combining human and zoomorphic features in a single compressed, upright composition — a form characteristic of ritual amulets of the late Neolithic Hongshan jade-working tradition. The composition follows a tightly stacked vertical logic: a raised crest or horn surmounts a broad, rounded head with enlarged eye sockets; below it, a simplified anthropomorphic torso shows an emphasised chest area and schematised limbs; the base is pierced by two drilled perforations, generally interpreted as attachment or suspension points.

The surface is partially overlaid by a dense calcite crust that softens the carved relief and fills the incised grooves — a pattern consistent with prolonged burial in an alkaline soil environment. This crust is especially visible along the ridges of the torso (Fig. 4).

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Characteristics

  • Mineral: nephrite (tremolite–actinolite)
  • Colour: grey-green, with brownish zones of natural oxidation
  • Patina: dense calcite encrustation with uneven crystallisation
  • Microstructure: microcracks, cavities, and zones of secondary mineral infill
  • Working: traces of abrasive carving and hand-drilling consistent with stone-tool working
  • Surface: no visible indication of modern mechanical tooling, polymer coatings, or restoration

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the calcite crust visible on this object (Fig. 4) is consistent with a mineralised secondary surface layer formed over a long burial interval: the crust fills the carved grooves rather than sitting as a superficial film, and its distribution is uneven — concentrated along ridges and raised carved elements rather than following any decorative logic. As with RO-JADE-001, no microscopy or instrumental analysis (SEM, XRF) has been carried out on this specific object for the present entry; the description above is a macroscopic, visual reading, not a laboratory-confirmed result.

Section 3

Cultural and Symbolic Context

The iconography of the object is consistent with the late Neolithic belief system associated with the Hongshan culture, within which carved jade images are generally understood to have functioned as intermediaries between worlds. Read against this interpretive background, the object's imagery combines several recurring themes:

  • Anthropo-zoomorphic fusion — a transitional form between a human figure and a guardian or spirit being.
  • Vertical structure — read as a symbol of connection between earth and sky.
  • Basal perforations — a functional element for ritual suspension, possibly for wear as an amulet or inclusion within a burial assemblage.
  • Crest or horn — a marker of sacred potency or spiritual hierarchy within this interpretive tradition.

Comparable forms are reported among finds from Liaoning and adjacent regions associated with shamanic ancestor cults of the period.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence in Section 2, not as an independent proof of date or function.
