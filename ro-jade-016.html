RO-JADE-016 · Late Zhou Ritual/Utilitarian Jade

Circular Carved Jade Box with Pierced Cover

Pale nephrite with diffuse russet-yellow patina, monolithic lidded box with openwork cover, Shaanxi or Henan

Pale Nephrite (Russet-Yellow Patina) Late Zhou, Warring States, c. 475–300 BCE Lidded Box, Openwork Cover Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object belongs to a category of small ritual-utilitarian jade sculpture of the late Zhou period, worked as a circular box with a closely fitted lid. Per the accompanying documentation, the box is carved from a single monolithic block of nephrite, with the lid and body cut from the same piece rather than assembled from separate elements.

The lid carries an elaborate system of relief and openwork carving, based on cloud-scroll (yunwen) motifs combined with stylised zoomorphic elements. Per the documentation, the perforation is read as serving both a decorative and a functional role, allowing air circulation and the possible diffusion of aromatic substances. The interior is hollowed with a high degree of regularity and finished with a matte polish, which the documentation reads as indicating a utilitarian use for the object.

Per the accompanying documentation, boxes of this type are interpreted in the archaeological literature as containers for incense, cosmetic substances, ritual powders, or aromatic compounds; within Warring States burial tradition, such objects are also read as carrying a symbolic function as a "vessel for the soul," accompanying the deceased into the afterlife. This page presents these readings as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense tremolite-type nephrite of medium translucency, without through-cracks or major structural defects.
  • Colour and structure: a transitional colour range from a creamy white to a warm amber-yellow, attributed to oxidative mineralisation involving iron (Fe³⁺) penetrating micro-cracks and porous zones of the structure.
  • Fabric: a fine-fibrous structure with localised zones of recrystallisation and micro-porous inclusions, described as the principal sites of secondary mineral and patina accumulation.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving of high engineering precision, including deep, even perforation of the lid along curved channels, precise fitting of lid to body with minimal gap, a symmetrically formed interior cavity, and retention of the monolithic integrity of the block — a technical level the documentation associates with developed late Zhou lapidary traditions.
  • Patina and wear: an even, diffuse russet-yellow patina, most pronounced in the carved recesses, along the inner edges of the lid, and in zones of micro-porosity and the lid/body join; the patina is described as having a natural gradient character, read as the result of prolonged interaction between the nephrite and a soil-geochemical environment. No signs of modern mechanical intervention, restoration, or artificial patination are noted in the documentation.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported patina distribution — concentrated in the carved recesses, inner lid edges, and porous or joining zones, with a natural gradient character — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, small lidded jade boxes of this type occupied a position between utilitarian and ritual use within late Zhou material culture, serving practical storage functions while also carrying symbolic associations within burial practice.

  • the monolithic construction as a marker of lapidary skill and material economy, per the documentation's reading;
  • the pierced, cloud-scroll cover as combining decorative and functional roles — ventilation and the possible diffusion of aromatic substances;
  • the object's reading, within Warring States burial tradition, as a "vessel for the soul" accompanying the deceased, alongside its plainer identification as a container for incense, cosmetic, or ritual substances.

Taken together, per the documentation, the box is read as an example of small-scale late Zhou jade craftsmanship in which utilitarian form and ritual-symbolic function were understood as combined within a single object.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
