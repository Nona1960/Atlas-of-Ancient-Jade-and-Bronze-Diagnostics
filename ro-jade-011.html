RO-JADE-011 · Eastern Zhou Ritual Jade

Ritual Rhinoceros Figure with Taotie Ornament

Nephrite with zonal iron-rich patina, zoomorphic guardian form, Yellow River basin

Nephrite (Zonal Patina) Eastern Zhou, Warring States Zoomorphic Guardian Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a rare example of jade zoomorphic sculpture, carved in the form of a rhinoceros, or a related guardian beast. The composition is massive and static, with an emphasis on stability rather than movement, a treatment consistent, per the accompanying documentation, with apotropaic ritual figures of the Eastern Zhou period.

The overall modelling favours weight and volume over naturalistic detail: the body is rendered as a generalised, symbolically dense mass rather than an anatomical study. The head is treated more sparingly than the body, with emphasis placed on silhouette, the horns, and the volumetric construction of the skull. This contrast between a restrained head and a densely ornamented body reinforces the object's reading as a guardian figure. The decorative programme is concentrated on the body and includes taotie-derived mask motifs, scrolling yunwen (cloud-scroll) ornament, concentric "eye" elements, and abstract linear signs recalling early forms of ritual proto-script, comparable in character to precursors of dazhuan seal script.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: tremolite-type nephrite, fine-grained, pale base tone.
  • Colour and structure: lighter tones concentrated around the head and muzzle, contrasting with a brown-black patina across the body; zones of hydroxide-related alteration are present.
  • Patina and ageing: natural, multi-layered patina with deep penetration into the micro-porous surface structure; brown-black discoloration associated with concentrations of iron and manganese compounds; fine dark residue within incised lines, possibly lacquer remnants or humic/carbonaceous material of undetermined composition.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand abrasive carving with variable incision depth in the ornament; ancient surface finishing with hard mineral abrasives; erosional softening of edges, leaching in the lower zones near the legs, and scattered micro-pitting consistent with long-term burial or age-related deterioration.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — zonal patina development, iron/manganese-related discoloration, and edge-softening consistent with prolonged burial — is consistent with a mineralised surface layer formed over a long interval. The head-and-muzzle detail (Fig. 2) is consistent with the zonal surface contrast described in the documentation.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, within Eastern Zhou tradition the rhinoceros was associated with strength, protection, and absolute stability. In ritual settings, figures of this kind are generally understood to have served several functions simultaneously:

  • the image of a guardian spirit, watching over burial chambers, temples, or other sacred spaces;
  • an apotropaic device, its taotie-derived ornament drawing on the older bronze-vessel tradition in which mask-like motifs were used to repel malevolent forces;
  • a concentration point for ritual potency, expressed through the combination of animal form and inherited bronze-derived decorative vocabulary.

The figure is read, per the documentation, as a hybrid guardian image, combining the physical authority of the rhinoceros with the symbolic apparatus of early Chinese ritual ornament, and situating the piece within a broader current of Eastern Zhou ritual art in which jade carving absorbed and reinterpreted motifs first developed for cast bronze vessels.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date or function.
