RO-JADE-013 · Late Zhou – Early Han Ritual Jade

Ritual Figure of a Winged Creature with Avian Head

White Hetian-type nephrite with ochre-brown mineralised surface, detachable-head construction, Yellow River basin

White Nephrite (Hetian Type) Late Zhou – Early Han, c. 3rd c. BCE Winged Avian-Headed Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a ritual jade figure carved in the form of a mythological winged creature with an avian head. Per the accompanying documentation, the combination of zoomorphic and fantastical features is characteristic of early sacred sculpture from northern China, in which a mythological image carries a ritual and symbolic function rather than a strictly naturalistic one.

The head is stylised in a manner associated, per the documentation, with early phoenix-related imagery: a pronounced almond-shaped eye, relief feather-scale ornament, and an elongated beak directed forward and upward. The body is massive, with smoothly continuous forms and a dense plastic structure. The back is carved with a complex system of scrolling ornament typical of late Zhou decorative composition, subordinated to the object's overall sculptural logic rather than serving a purely decorative role.

A notable feature of the construction is the detachable head, apparently seated on a tenon-type joint (Fig. 3). Per the accompanying documentation, this is read as suggesting a more complex functional character for the object, consistent with use either as a ritual implement with a possible internal sacred function, or as a container-type amulet piece; this page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: white Hetian-type nephrite, a dense fibrous aggregate of tremolite and actinolite; the original material colour is reconstructed as milky white with a soft translucency characteristic of higher-quality nephrite of this type.
  • Colour and structure: the present surface shows a warm ochre-yellow tone with localised orange-brown inclusions, read in the documentation as the result of long-term iron- and phosphate-related mineralisation in a soil environment.
  • Patina and ageing: a microscopically uneven surface structure, with zones of natural micro-porosity, micro-cracks partially infilled with secondary iron hydroxides, and areas of retained original silky-matte lustre within the recessed carving.
  • Manufacturing technology: high-precision carving with deep plastic modelling of details, micro-undercutting in hard-to-reach zones, and complex openwork perforation; working marks are consistent with traditional mineral abrasive materials, without indications of mechanised finishing such as uniform grinding or synthetic polishing.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported distribution of secondary mineralisation — concentrated in the recessed carving and ornamental elements, at the head/body joint, along micro-cracks and opened fibre zones, and in areas that would be hard to reach mechanically — is described in the documentation as structurally consistent with the fibrous nature of nephrite and with prolonged interaction with a soil-derived geochemical environment. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the winged, avian-headed form belongs to a category of ritual jade sculpture in which a mythological composite image carries ritual and symbolic significance within the sacred sculptural tradition of northern China. The stylisation of the head, with its feather-scale ornament and forward-directed beak, is read as drawing on early phoenix-related iconography.

  • the composite winged, avian-headed form as an embodiment of a mythological, rather than purely animal, being;
  • the scrolling back ornament as an extension of late Zhou decorative composition into a symbolically charged sculptural surface;
  • the detachable-head construction as a possible marker of a more complex ritual or container-related function, per the documentation's reading.

Taken together, per the documentation, the object is read as belonging to the tradition of ritual jade sculpture of the late Zhou period, in which material, form, and constructional complexity combine to express a symbolic and ceremonial function attributed to jade objects of this kind.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
