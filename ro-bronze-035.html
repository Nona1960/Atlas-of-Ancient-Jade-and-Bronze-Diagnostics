01 Object Description and Attribution

The vessel is a bronze ritual wine vessel of zoomorphic (zun/fangzun) type, cast in the form of a standing deer on four legs, with a raised head and neck and paired antler-like projections at the crown. The body is hollow and forms the vessel proper, with a mouth (spout) opening at the haunch (Fig. 5). A fitted lid is set into the back over the body cavity, itself surmounted by a small bird figure cast separately and mounted at the lid’s centre (Fig. 2). The body surface carries continuous low-relief ornament combining spiral (whorl) motifs and taotie-derived mask elements, arranged in registers across the shoulder, haunch, and flank (Figs. 1, 3, 4). Surface condition shows a well-developed, unevenly coloured archaeological patina — malachite-green corrosion zones alongside brown-ochre oxidation — consistent with long-term natural burial.

The vessel is attributed to the late Shang period, ca. 13th–11th century BCE, on typological and stylistic grounds: form, ornament, and casting characteristics were assessed by the author against published Shang zoomorphic bronzes and discussed in consultation with Alexander Kosolapov, Head of the Department of Scientific-Technological Expertise, State Hermitage Museum. This typological attribution is fully consistent with, and not contradicted by, the metallurgical evidence set out below.

02 SEM–EDX Diagnostic Analysis (Ten-Point Survey)

The metallurgical dataset combines two independently dated SEM–EDX sampling campaigns carried out by the author on 19 January 2017 — one on the vessel’s base zone, one on the lid/rim zone — together with a supplementary matrix point and a dedicated analysis of an internal patina deposit. Across all ten points, the alloy is a coherent, internally consistent archaic copper–tin bronze with antimony, arsenic, and heterogeneously distributed lead (Cu–Sn–Sb–As–Pb); no zinc is present at any of the ten points.

SEM imaging of the internal patina deposit shows white, loose, cauliflower-like crystalline aggregates concentrated in corners and recesses — consistent with secondary chloride/sodium salt formation from prolonged contact with burial soil. The trace rhodium reading is an instrumental artefact (rhodium is a standard anode material in EDX systems) and is not part of the sample composition.

2.4 Alloy interpretation

Six of the ten points are dominated by lead at 90%+ with correspondingly depressed copper — the SEM beam landing directly on discrete, undissolved lead-globule inclusions, a well-documented feature of leaded archaic bronze in which lead segregates as a separate phase on cooling. The four matrix-proper points cluster tightly around Cu 78–80%, Sn 8–10%, Sb 1.7–2.2%, As up to 2.7%, and Pb 1.6–4.0% — a coherent archaic alloy recipe, with antimony and arsenic co-occurrence indicating unrefined, complex-ore pyrometallurgy consistent with the proposed period.

03 Iconography and Cultural Context

The pairing of a deer-form vessel body with a bird-form finial belongs to the Shang and early Zhou tradition of zoomorphic ritual bronzes, in which real and composite animal forms carried symbolic associations connected to ancestor worship and the ritual presentation of wine offerings. The taotie-derived mask ornament and spiral registers on the body are consistent with the decorative vocabulary of the period. The vessel’s casting — separately made lid and finial fitted to a cast body — reflects the multi-stage piece-mould construction characteristic of Shang bronze workshops.

04 Conclusion

Ten independently obtained SEM–EDX points across two sampling sessions and two vessel zones establish a coherent, internally consistent archaic Cu–Sn–Sb–As–Pb bronze alloy with heterogeneously distributed lead, fully consistent with late Shang metallurgical practice. This composition, together with the vessel’s form, casting technique, and ornament programme, supports the attribution of the piece to the late Shang period, ca. 13th–11th century BCE.
