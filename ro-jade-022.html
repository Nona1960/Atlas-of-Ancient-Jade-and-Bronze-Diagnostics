RO-JADE-022 · Late Eastern Zhou Ritual Jade

Winged Dragon Amulet in White/Yellow-Gray Jade

Dense yellow-gray Hetian-type nephrite with russet-ochre patina, low-relief winged dragon pendant, Yellow River basin

Yellow-Gray Nephrite (Russet-Ochre Patina) Late Eastern Zhou, Warring States, c. 475–300 BCE Winged Dragon Amulet Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a finely carved winged mythical creature, rendered in low relief from a single piece of jade. Per the accompanying documentation, the catalogue reference for this object is WSH-09, and the piece is read as a pendant or amulet.

Per the documentation, the composition includes a prominent wing with a sharply defined rib, a stylised muzzle, and curled motifs on the legs; a small hole pierces the upper section, read as intended for suspension or hanging. The carving is shallow but precise, with soft abrasive polishing; a natural russet-brown patina is noted as concentrated in the grooves and along the edges.

Per the accompanying documentation, the style is read as combining Bronze-Age totemic imagery with the decorative vocabulary of the Warring States period; this page presents that stylistic reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an independently verified attribution.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: tremolitic nephrite with local epidote-related zones, described as showing a fibrous "sheaf-like" structure noted as characteristic of Hetian-type jade.
  • Colour and patina: a natural russet-ochre patina concentrated in grooves and carved edges, penetrating deep into micro-cracks and incised lines.
  • Residual deposits: a compact residue described as archaeological cement, composed of silica, clays, and iron oxides, noted as remaining fused within the recesses.
  • Manufacturing technology: fine hand carving with soft polish; the documentation notes no indication of modern mechanical or chemical treatment.

The documentation states that previous SEM-EDX analyses of comparable specimens — not of this specific object — showed a chemical signature (O, Si, Fe, Al, Mg, C) consistent with long-term natural burial. This page records that claim as reported, but notes explicitly that no instrumental analysis of this particular object is documented; the SEM-EDX results referenced concern other, comparable pieces, not this one.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — patina penetration into carved lines, fused archaeological residue, and a fibrous internal texture — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. The SEM-EDX data cited in the documentation refer to comparable specimens, not to this object, and are reported here as such rather than as direct analytical evidence for this piece. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the motif of a winged dragon, combined with sophisticated low-relief and openwork carving, is read as consistent with high-status amulets of the Warring States period. The documentation notes comparable jade pendants and ornaments reported from Henan, Shaanxi, and Beijing collections, where such pieces are interpreted as aristocratic symbols of celestial protection and ritual power.

  • the winged dragon form as a symbol of protection, power, and celestial connection, per the documentation's reading;
  • the suspension hole as evidence of a wearable or hangable amuletic function;
  • the combination of totemic imagery and Warring States decorative language as situating the piece within an elite ritual and ornamental tradition.

Taken together, per the documentation, the amulet is read as an example of Warring States aristocratic jade ornament in which protective symbolism and refined low-relief carving combine within a single wearable object.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
