RO-JADE-017 · Eastern Zhou Ritual Jade

Small Ritual Tripod Vessel with Lid and Beast-Head Handles

Dense white nephrite with ochre-brown patina, tripod lidded vessel, Yellow River basin

White Nephrite (Ochre-Brown Patina) Eastern Zhou, Warring States, c. 4th–3rd c. BCE Tripod Lidded Vessel Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a compact tripod ritual vessel with a lid, worked in dense white nephrite. Per the accompanying documentation, the piece is read as a status-related ritual vessel — an incense burner or offering vessel of the kind associated with aristocratic ceremony — showing a high degree of plastic and symbolic elaboration for its small scale.

The body rests on three rounded, stable legs forming a tripod configuration. Miniature handles in the form of stylised mythological beast heads flank the sides of the vessel; per the documentation, this is read as carrying protective, apotropaic symbolism. The lid is dome-shaped and finished with a central hemispherical rosette-form knob, seated with close precision on the vessel's short neck.

Per the accompanying documentation, the ornamental system includes softly rendered geometric bands that recall the decorative tradition of bronze ritual ding vessels, read as linking this small jade piece to the broader state and cultic symbolism of the period. This page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established fact.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense white nephrite, possibly of Hetian type.
  • Colour and structure: a natural ochre-brown to russet-yellow patina formed, per the documentation, through prolonged natural ageing.
  • Patina and ageing: even natural ageing with deep penetration of iron-siliceous compounds into the carved micro-relief; a surface structure without indications of modern-type mechanical working, retaining a soft, aged polish.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving with a high degree of precision in the fitting of the vessel's components, and a stable tripod base construction that the documentation associates with high-quality pieces of the 4th–3rd century BCE.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — deep patina penetration into the carved recesses, a soft aged surface polish, and stable tripod construction — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the tripod form, beast-head handles, and rosette lid motif situate the object within the elite ritual tradition of the Warring States period. Comparable pieces are noted in the documentation as known from archaeological assemblages in Henan, Shanxi, and Shandong, where jade vessels of this kind were used in private and temple ceremonies connected with ancestor veneration and aromatic offerings.

  • the beast-head handles as an expression of protective, apotropaic symbolism, per the documentation's reading;
  • the tripod form and geometric bands as a link to the bronze ding-vessel tradition and its associated state and cultic symbolism;
  • the object's small scale and precise construction as evidence, per the documentation, of its status as a refined ceremonial piece rather than a purely utilitarian container.

Taken together, per the documentation, the vessel is read as a high-quality example of small-scale Warring States ritual jade, in which material, form, and surface condition combine to express both a ceremonial function and elite status.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
