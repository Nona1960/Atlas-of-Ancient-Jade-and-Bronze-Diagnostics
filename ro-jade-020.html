RO-JADE-020 · Late Zhou Ritual Jade

Recumbent Figure of a Horned Animal (Ram) with Curled Horns

White and pale yellow nephrite with ochre-brown and russet-gold patina, recumbent caprid form, Yellow River basin

White/Pale Yellow Nephrite Late Zhou, Warring States, c. 475–221 BCE Recumbent Horned Animal Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a jade figure of a horned animal, read per the accompanying documentation as a ram or a symbolically related horned creature, carved from a single block in a compact, stable, recumbent composition.

Per the documentation, the head shows ears pressed to the skull and massive, smoothly curled horns forming a rhythmic arc; the muzzle is elongated, with small, carefully indicated eyes. The neck is raised vertically, read as conveying alertness and inner strength. The body is massive, with a soft plastic modelling of the chest and torso, and the legs are stable and generalised, without detailed anatomical articulation.

Per the accompanying documentation, the figure is catalogued under the reference WSH-04; the documentation's own title for this reference ("Covered Censer") does not correspond to the object shown in the photographs, which is a solid recumbent figure without a lid or cavity. This page follows the object as photographed and describes it as a horned-animal figure, not as a censer.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: white and pale yellow nephrite, possibly of Hetian type.
  • Colour and structure: a natural ochre-brown and russet-gold patina, described as deeply penetrating micro-cracks and carved recesses, with intensification in relief zones and areas of subsurface defect; a slight translucency is noted at the base of the horns and in the abdominal area, read as an "inner glow" effect.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand abrasive carving with soft, smoothed transitions of volume, shallow but confident modelling of form, careful polishing producing a soft silky lustre, and retention of the monolithic integrity of the original block.
  • Patina and wear: a dense, even reddish-brown patina covering the whole surface, with natural mineral inclusions and micro-cracks consistent, per the documentation, with archaic Hetian-type nephrite. No indications of mechanised drilling or modern tools are noted.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — a deeply penetrating natural patina, subsurface translucency at the horn base and abdomen, and natural mineral inclusions — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the horned animal in this context is read as a status-related ritual figure — a symbol of strength and guardianship used in aristocratic ceremony and personal ritual practice — and the generalised plasticity, raised-head pose, and emphasis on the horns are noted as consistent with the ritual jade tradition of the late Eastern Zhou, Warring States period.

  • the horned animal as a symbol of strength and martial valour, per the documentation's reading;
  • the figure as a protective emblem of aristocratic status;
  • the animal's reading as a mediator between the terrestrial and celestial realms, within the broader symbolic vocabulary of Zhou ritual jade.

Taken together, per the documentation, the figure is read as an example of late Zhou ritual jade sculpture in which a compact zoomorphic form and symbolically charged horn treatment combine to express ideas of strength, status, and protection.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
