RO-JADE-001 · Neolithic Ritual Jade

Ritual Figure of a Winged Spirit in Dark Nephrite

Black amphibole nephrite carving, splayed-wing chiropteran (bat) form

Dark Nephrite Hongshan-Related Neolithic Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) Serpentinization

Section 1

Object Description

The object is a small carved figure in dark (near-black) nephrite, rendered as a winged creature with a broad, rounded head, prominent circular eyes, and a pair of symmetrically outstretched wings folded into a compact, frontal composition. The stance is frontal and hieratic, with the wings forming the outer silhouette and the short legs drawn together beneath the body. The overall form is consistent with the "splayed-wing chiropteran" (bat-form) type documented among Chinese Neolithic and early historic jade carvings.

The surface is not uniformly dark: across the wings and body, pale grey-to-white mottled patches interrupt the dark matrix in an irregular, cloud-like distribution, concentrated toward the outer wing surfaces and high points of the carving. This two-tone patterning — a dark base material overlaid by lighter secondary patches — is the visible macroscopic signature discussed in the diagnostic section below. The dorsal (back) surface (Fig. 2) shows the same pale mottling concentrated along the central ridge of the back and across both wing surfaces, with the underside of the wing tips comparatively less affected — a distribution consistent with differential exposure of the carving's high points within the burial environment.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Material Genesis and Surface Transformation

Dark nephrite of the type seen in this object is an amphibole nephrite (Fe-rich actinolite–tremolite), formed under low-temperature metamorphism and used in ritual assemblages of north-eastern China. Its dense, fibrous, dark microstructure makes it a diagnostically sensitive material: prolonged burial produces a predictable, staged sequence of surface alteration that can be read as a relative indicator of age.

The Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) method reconstructs this sequence in four stages, from the unaltered material to the stabilised, archaeologically aged surface observed today:

1

Original state — unaltered dark nephrite

Dense, fibrous amphibole aggregate; uniform dark colouration; microcracks absent or minimal; sharp, unreacted boundary between the material and the surrounding soil.

2

Onset of corrosion in the burial environment

Diffusion of moisture and oxygen into surface microcracks; weak oxidation of iron-bearing phases; a thin reaction zone begins to form, and the artefact–soil boundary becomes transitional rather than sharp.

3

Mineralisation of the surface layer (serpentinization)

Partial replacement of amphibole by serpentine and other secondary minerals, with hydration and the formation of pale phases. The upper layer becomes heterogeneous, showing white to light-grey serpentinite-type patches against the dark matrix, while the fibrous texture is retained; the boundary is diffusional, grading gradually into the mineralised zone.

4

Stabilised archaeological state

The object retains its carved form and fibrous structure; the upper layer is corroded and mineralised (serpentinite-type patches), while the interior remains largely unaltered dark nephrite. This combination — dark core, pale-mottled surface layer — is read as an indicator of prolonged burial over a millennial timescale.

On the present object, the macroscopic pale mottling visible across the wings and upper surfaces (Figs. 1–2) is consistent with Stage 3–4 of this model: a dark, largely unaltered core material with a mineralised, lighter-toned surface layer concentrated on the exposed high points of the carving. No microscopy or instrumental analysis (SEM, XRF) has been carried out on this specific object for the present entry; the description above is a macroscopic, visual reading against the STA framework, not a laboratory-confirmed result.

Serpentinization — the hydration and mineralogical alteration of Mg/Fe-silicate material — is a well-documented general geological process. Its specific rate and expression in a buried nephrite artefact depend on local burial conditions (soil chemistry, moisture, temperature) and are not identical to serpentinization in other geological settings (e.g. oceanic crust). The staged model above describes burial-environment alteration of the artefact, not a laboratory-dated rate curve.

Section 3

Cultural and Symbolic Context

In early Chinese ritual and shamanic traditions — including those of north-eastern Neolithic cultures — the image of the bat, or more broadly of a "night-winged being," did not carry a single fixed meaning in the modern moral sense of "good" or "evil." It functioned instead as an image tied to transitional and liminal states of the world. Read against this ethnographic background, dark winged-spirit figures are generally associated with several overlapping ideas:

Night as a threshold. In shamanic cosmology, night is not merely a time of day but a space in which the boundaries between worlds become permeable. A being associated with night could be understood as a mediator between the living and ancestral realms — a liminal image rather than a frightening one.

Protection through potency and otherness. Dark totemic figures are often understood to have carried an apotropaic function: protection enacted through a powerful, not-fully-legible image. The logic is that what appears potent and partly unknowable deters destructive forces — an image of spatial control rather than of "positive" or "negative" energy as such.

Liminal potency. Such beings belong to the category of "threshold spirits," fully at home neither in the sky nor on the earth. Their potency lies in their capacity to exist between states — life and death, light and dark, human and spirit.

Possible ritual function. Within an altar or burial context, a figure of this type may have served as a guardian of space, a psychopomp guiding the soul, or a symbolic "seal" restricting the access of harmful forces.

The later, better-known association of the bat (蝠, fú) with good fortune — arising from its phonetic resemblance to the word for "blessing" (福, fú) — belongs to a considerably later, more rationalised layer of Chinese culture. For an object of the attributed Neolithic horizon, the threshold/protective reading above is the more relevant interpretive frame; the later "good fortune" association is noted here only for completeness and should not be read back onto this object's original context.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence in Section 2, not as an independent proof of date or function.
