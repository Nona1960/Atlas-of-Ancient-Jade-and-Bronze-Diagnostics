Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)
& Trace Element Tail Analysis (TETA)

An integrated methodological system for the non-destructive diagnostics of ancient jade and bronze artefacts

Developed by Professor Nona Dronova · Doctor of Technical Sciences · Ph.D. in Geology and Mineralogy

Method overview

Under the scientific direction of Professor Nona Dronova, an integrated methodological system for the non-destructive diagnostics of ancient artefacts has been established, based on the analysis of the long-term natural processes of their structural and geochemical evolution. The system comprises two material-specific methods — STA for jade and TETA for bronze — unified by a single diagnostic philosophy.

Chapter 1

Methodology of Diagnostics of Ancient Chinese Jade

1.1 Theoretical Foundations of Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Authenticating ancient Chinese jade artefacts is one of the most difficult tasks in contemporary museum, archaeological, and collecting practice. Despite the wide use of mineralogical, spectroscopic, and chemical-analytical methods, a large share of research still focuses on determining the mineral composition of the jade, its textural features, and the source of the raw material. These characteristics alone, however, cannot reliably establish the antiquity of an object's manufacture.

The use of genuinely ancient jade raw material in modern fakes, the re-working of archaeological blanks, and the artificial ageing of surfaces all significantly limit the diagnostic value of mineral-composition analysis alone. Because of this, the true object of study becomes not the mineral itself, but the patterns of natural transformation of its surface, occurring over the object's long residence in an archaeological environment.

The Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) method is based on a fundamentally different approach to the study of ancient jade. Its focus is the artefact's surface layer, treated as an independent natural-historical system that changes progressively under the physical, chemical, mineralogical, and biological processes of its surrounding environment.

After an object is made, its surface begins to interact with groundwater, dissolved mineral components, weathering products, microorganisms, and the soil's gas environment. These processes continue for centuries or millennia and lead to the gradual formation of a new surface layer, substantially different in structure from the original surface that existed immediately after polishing.

It is precisely this secondary layer that carries the most valuable information about the artefact's history. During prolonged archaeological exposure, secondary minerals form on the surface; selective dissolution of individual areas occurs; microporosity develops; the character of the microrelief changes; zones of recrystallisation and local iron and manganese accumulations arise; and complex spatial structures form, reflecting the successive stages of the material's natural ageing.

Unlike artificially created patina or modern ageing-imitation technologies, natural surface transformation is the result of extremely slow geochemical processes occurring under conditions that cannot be fully reproduced over a short period of time. This is precisely why the study of secondary surface changes carries far greater diagnostic value than an assessment of chemical composition or decorative features alone.

The STA method treats the surface of ancient jade as an archive of the object's natural geochemical history. Each stage of the mineral's interaction with its environment leaves characteristic microscopic traces, the totality of which allows the duration of the processes to be reconstructed, the sequence of secondary-change formation to be determined, and the probability of the object's archaeological origin to be assessed.

Within the method, attention is directed not at any single feature but at the analysis of the whole complex of interrelated surface transformations. This approach substantially reduces the likelihood of misinterpreting individual diagnostic features and allows an object's antiquity to be assessed on the basis of the regularities of the natural evolution of its surface layer.

Core definition

Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) is not a single analytical technique but a comprehensive scientific methodology for the non-destructive diagnosis of ancient jade artefacts, based on the microscopic study of the long-term transformation of their surfaces — distinguishing genuine ancient objects from modern productions and artificial imitations.

1.2 Mineralogical Features of Nephrite as an Object of STA Diagnosis

The effectiveness of STA is determined by the unique mineralogical and structural features of nephrite. Unlike most ornamental stones, nephrite is not a single crystal but a natural polymineral aggregate composed of extremely fine, interwoven amphibole fibres. In ancient Chinese nephrite, the principal minerals are tremolite and actinolite, forming a dense spatial network of mutually interlaced microfibres.

It is precisely this fibrous organisation that gives nephrite its exceptional mechanical strength while simultaneously creating a complex system of micropores, inter-fibre contacts, capillary channels, and crystal-structure defects. After an object has been worked — by sawing, drilling, carving, grinding, and especially polishing — some fibres are cut through, exposing numerous crystal end-faces. These areas are considerably more chemically reactive than the natural surfaces of unworked stone.

Polishing does not fully eliminate the surface microrelief. At the microscopic level, traces of working remain — exposed fibre ends, zones of plastic deformation, microcracks, and areas of localised disruption of the crystal structure. It is precisely these microscopic features that become the primary centres of the jade's subsequent interaction with the archaeological environment.

Once an object is placed in the ground, a prolonged process of physico-chemical interaction between its surface and the surrounding environment begins. Soil solutions, containing dissolved compounds of iron, manganese, silicon, calcium, aluminium and other elements, gradually penetrate the surface layer through the system of micropores and inter-fibre spaces. The rate of penetration is determined by fibre orientation, the degree of their exposure after working, the porosity of individual areas, and burial conditions.

The heterogeneity of the ancient jade surface plays a particular role. Areas subjected to intensive mechanical working have a considerably greater specific surface area than natural fractures or unworked zones. This produces differing rates of adsorption, sorption, and diffusion of chemical components, so that secondary minerals are distributed unevenly, forming a characteristic spatial zonation.

Studies of archaeological jade show that the maximum concentration of secondary iron formations is usually observed in areas with a high density of exposed fibre ends, in zones of ancient carving, around drilled holes, along polished facets, and in microcracks that arose even during the object's original manufacture. Such a distribution is a natural consequence of nephrite's internal structure and does not follow any decorative logic — a point that clearly distinguishes natural archaeological transformation from modern methods of artificial colouring.

Over centuries of interaction with the archaeological environment, the original surface gradually turns into a complex, multi-component system that includes the preserved primary mineral, products of its partial dissolution, secondary iron and manganese formations, and zones of microcrystallisation, leaching, and recrystallisation. Each of these components reflects a particular stage of the surface's natural evolution and carries its own diagnostic information.

Within STA, the jade surface is treated as a dynamic mineralogical system that has continuously changed throughout the object's entire archaeological history. The principal object of study therefore becomes not the chemical composition of the nephrite as such, but the sequence in which secondary surface structures formed as a result of prolonged geochemical processes.

Thus, the mineralogical structure of nephrite is not only the basis of its physical properties but also the principal factor determining the character of the long-term archaeological transformation of its surface. It is precisely this regular relationship between the mineral's internal structure and the evolution of its surface layer that forms the foundation of STA.

1.3 Physico-Chemical Mechanisms of Archaeological Surface Transformation

The long-term transformation of the nephrite surface under archaeological burial conditions is a complex set of interrelated physico-chemical, geochemical, and biogeochemical processes. Within STA, these processes are treated as the fundamental mechanism by which the diagnostically significant surface layer is formed.

1.3.1 General Scheme of Surface Evolution

After a nephrite artefact is made and subsequently enters the soil environment, a gradual restructuring of the mineral's surface zone begins. Initially, this restructuring is local in character and is linked to working defects — microcracks, exposed fibre ends, zones of plastic deformation, and disruptions of the crystal packing. Over time, the system of processes becomes stable and includes:

  • diffusion of dissolved ions from soil solutions;
  • adsorption of ions on reactive surfaces;
  • chemical interaction with the components of the nephrite;
  • precipitation of secondary minerals;
  • partial dissolution of the most reactive areas.

1.3.2 Migration of Iron and Manganese

A key role in the formation of diagnostically significant surface structures is played by the migration of iron and manganese compounds. In natural waters, these elements occur in dissolved forms, including ionic and complex compounds stable under weakly acidic and neutral conditions.

As soil solutions penetrate the jade's surface layer, Fe2+ and Fe3+ gradually accumulate in zones of increased permeability. As the redox potential of the environment changes, iron undergoes successive oxidation–reduction transformations, leading to the formation of stable secondary phases such as hematite (Fe₂O₃), goethite (FeOOH), and lepidocrocite (γ-FeOOH).

Manganese participates in analogous processes, forming oxide and hydroxide phases that often coexist with the iron formations and intensify the heterogeneity of the surface layer's colouration.

1.3.3 The Role of Nephrite Microstructure in Selective Mineralisation

The distribution pattern of secondary minerals is governed by nephrite's microstructure. The fibrous nature of the tremolite–actinolite aggregate produces a pronounced anisotropy in the physico-chemical properties of the surface. The most reactive zones are:

  • fibre end-faces;
  • areas subjected to mechanical working;
  • microcracks and cleavage defects;
  • zones of increased porosity formed during carving and grinding.

These are precisely the areas that show the greatest sorption capacity and become centres of selective accumulation of iron and manganese compounds. The result is a characteristic spatial heterogeneity of colouration, unrelated to any external artistic or technological cause.

1.3.4 Biogeochemical Factors

An important element of STA is accounting for biogeochemical processes. Soil microorganisms participate in the oxidation and reduction of iron and manganese, altering their valence state and, consequently, their solubility. Microbiological activity contributes to:

  • accelerating the oxidation of Fe2+ to Fe3+;
  • the formation of local zones of hydroxide precipitation;
  • the stabilisation of finely dispersed mineral phases on the jade surface.

The biological factor thus acts as a catalyst for the geochemical evolution of the surface layer, intensifying the complexity and multi-stage character of the transformation processes.

1.3.5 Stages in the Formation of the Secondary Surface Layer

Within STA, the following provisional stages of surface transformation are distinguished:

Stage I — Surface activationFormation of zones of increased reactivity as a result of mechanical working and micro-damage.
Stage II — Ionic infiltrationPenetration of dissolved components of soil solutions into the surface layer.
Stage III — Primary mineralisationFormation of amorphous and weakly crystalline iron and manganese compounds.
Stage IV — Crystallisation of secondary phasesFormation of stable minerals (hematite, goethite, lepidocrocite).
Stage V — Structural stabilisationEstablishment of a stable equilibrium between the primary and secondary mineral phases of the surface.

1.3.6 Significance for the STA Method

The totality of the processes described above forms a complex, multi-layered system of surface transformations, which is the key object of analysis in STA. It is of fundamental importance that these processes:

  • occur over a long period of time;
  • are multi-staged in character;
  • depend on the local microstructure of the material;
  • cannot be precisely reproduced under artificial conditions over a short time.

For this reason, the observed microstructure of the surface layer can be regarded as a reliable indicator of nephrite's prolonged presence in an archaeological environment, and serves as the basis for the scientific diagnosis of the authenticity of ancient artefacts.

1.4 Microscopic Diagnostic Criteria of Jade Antiquity (STA Criteria)

STA is based on identifying a set of microscopic features that form as a result of nephrite's prolonged interaction with the archaeological environment. Unlike visual-stylistic analysis, STA relies on the observation of structures inaccessible to the naked eye, recorded only at microscopic scale. The diagnostic value of any single feature is limited; however, their stable combinations and spatial regularities form a reliable basis for establishing an artefact's antiquity.

1.4.1 Secondary Iron-Mineral Formations

One of the most significant diagnostic features is the local accumulation of secondary iron minerals. Under the microscope, these appear as:

  • finely dispersed hematite aggregates;
  • platy and needle-like lepidocrocite crystals;
  • amorphous iron hydroxide films;
  • zones of gradual transition between the original jade surface and the secondary mineralisation.

A characteristic feature is the unevenness of distribution, caused by the structural anisotropy of the nephrite. Secondary formations concentrate predominantly in zones of exposed fibres, microcracks, and areas of ancient mechanical working.

1.4.2 Pseudo-Reticulate and Linear Structural Formations

Ancient jade is characterised by the formation of complex spatial structures, including:

  • pseudo-reticulate (net-like) iron formations;
  • linear chains of mineral inclusions;
  • branching hydroxide micronetworks;
  • structures oriented along the fibrous texture of the nephrite.

These formations reflect the directed migration of iron and manganese ions along microstructural channels and result from long-term diffusion and crystallisation processes.

1.4.3 Zones of Leaching and Structural Depletion

A characteristic feature of nephrite's prolonged presence in the archaeological environment is the presence of zones of partial leaching, manifested as:

  • localised reduction in optical density;
  • microporous areas with weakened structure;
  • areas of partial loss of the original mineralogical cohesion.

These zones frequently border areas of intensive secondary mineralisation, forming contrasting microchemical gradients.

1.4.4 Microrelief Associated with Prolonged Surface Transformation

Under the microscope, ancient jade surfaces show a complex relief that cannot be explained by working technique alone. It includes:

  • smoothed but structurally heterogeneous zones;
  • micro-stepped transitions between layers of transformation;
  • localised areas of recrystallisation;
  • areas of secondary "re-melting" of the microstructure.

Importantly, this relief has no regular character and does not follow instrumental symmetry — a feature that distinguishes it from modern mechanical treatment.

1.4.5 Spatial Consistency of Features

The key criterion of STA is not the presence of individual features but their spatial consistency. Ancient jade is characterised by an interconnected system in which:

  • zones of secondary mineralisation coincide with areas of mechanical working;
  • iron accumulations correlate with microcracks and fibre end-faces;
  • leaching and mineralisation form conjugate structures.

This consistency reflects the natural evolution of the surface and results from the long-term geochemical development of the system.

1.4.6 Diagnostic Significance of STA Criteria

The set of microscopic features described above forms the basis of the diagnostic conclusion within STA. It is of fundamental importance that the assessment of authenticity is not based on isolated observations, but is built on an analysis of the entire system of surface transformations as a single process.

Artificial imitations typically show:

  • local inconsistency of features;
  • absence of deep structural evolution;
  • superficial colouration without staged formation.

By contrast, genuine ancient jade artefacts are characterised by a multi-level, spatially connected, and morphologically complex system of surface transformation, formed over a long period under natural geochemical conditions.

Thus, the microscopic STA criteria constitute the principal tool for identifying the antiquity of nephrite and allow the transition from subjective visual analysis to objective structural diagnosis.

Chapter 2

Methodology of Diagnostics of Ancient Chinese Bronzes — Trace Element Tail Analysis (TETA)

2.1 Theoretical Foundations of TETA

Trace Element Tail Analysis (TETA) is a non-destructive geochemical approach to diagnosing ancient Chinese bronzes, based on analysing the distribution of trace-element impurities and their "tail" concentration profiles within the structure of the alloy.

Unlike traditional metallographic and spectral diagnostics, which focus on the averaged composition of the metal, TETA treats bronze as the product of processing natural ores that retain complex and heterogeneous geochemical signatures. These signatures do not disappear entirely during smelting but are redistributed as stable trace-element associations.

Core proposition

Ancient bronze is not a "pure alloy" in the modern technological sense, but a hereditary ore-metallurgical system in which traces of the original mineral raw material are preserved.

2.2 The Geochemical Nature of Ancient Chinese Bronzes

Ancient Chinese bronzes were formed predominantly through the processing of complex polymetallic ores containing not only copper and tin but also substantial amounts of associated elements, including lead, antimony, arsenic, iron, nickel, and rare trace elements.

A feature of early metallurgical technology was the absence of deep refining of the raw material, which led to a wide range of impurity elements being retained in the alloy. The result was a complex structure in which the principal metallic matrix coexists with stable geochemical "traces" of the original minerals. These traces manifest not only in the bulk composition of the metal but also in the micro-heterogeneities of element distribution, forming what are termed trace element tails.

2.3 The Concept of Trace Element "Tails" and Their Diagnostic Significance

Within TETA, "tails" of trace elements are understood as stable, low-concentration distributions of impurities that retain a link to their original ore associations. Unlike the uniformly distributed elements of modern refined metal, ancient bronzes show:

  • local zones of elevated antimony and arsenic concentration;
  • associations of copper with iron and nickel;
  • uneven distribution of lead within the matrix;
  • stable correlations between elements characteristic of specific ore types.

These "tails" result from the incomplete breakdown of the original raw material's mineralogical structure and reflect the geological history of the ore used in smelting.

2.4 The Relationship Between Trace Element Composition and Patina Formation

Of particular importance in TETA is the analysis of the relationship between the bulk composition of the alloy and the chemical-mineralogical composition of the patina. The patina of ancient bronzes is treated not as an external corrosion shell, but as a continuation of the alloy's geochemical system, forming as a result of the metal's prolonged interaction with its environment. During corrosion, the following occur:

  • selective leaching of copper;
  • relative enrichment of the surface layer in tin and lead;
  • migration of trace elements into the oxidation zone;
  • fixation of antimony, arsenic, and iron in the secondary minerals of the patina.

The patina thus becomes a carrier of redistributed geochemical information originally embedded in the structure of the alloy.

2.5 The Significance of TETA in Diagnosing Ancient Bronzes

TETA allows an ancient bronze to be treated as an integrated "ore — alloy — patina" system, in which each stage retains interrelated geochemical traces. The key diagnostic principle is not the absolute content of elements, but their:

  • associative relationships;
  • distribution within micro-volumes;
  • consistency between alloy and patina;
  • the presence of stable ore signatures.

It is precisely this systemic structure that allows ancient bronzes, formed from natural ores with minimal refining, to be distinguished from modern imitations made from refined metals with artificially created patina.

2.6 Microscopic and Analytical Criteria of Bronze Authenticity (TETA Criteria)

TETA is based on identifying a set of microanalytical features reflecting the ore origin of the alloy and the long-term geochemical evolution of the "metal — environment" system. Authentication is carried out not through single compositional parameters, but through stable elemental associations, their distribution, and their relationship to corrosion products.

2.6.1 Trace Element Associations as the Basis of Diagnosis

Ancient Chinese bronzes are characterised by stable geochemical associations reflecting the original ore nature of the raw material. The most significant combinations are:

  • Cu–Sn–Pb as the principal metallurgical system;
  • Cu–As–Sb as an indicator of polymetallic ores;
  • Fe–Ni–Co as trace ore components;
  • localised inclusions of rare elements linked to natural mineralisation.

An important diagnostic feature is not only the presence of these elements, but their joint spatial distribution, which preserves correlations characteristic of the original minerals.

2.6.2 The Character of Trace Element "Tails"

Within TETA, "tails" are understood as low-concentration zones of element distribution that retain a link to ore genetics. Ancient bronzes are characterised by:

  • a smooth decay in element concentration from inclusions toward the matrix;
  • the presence of micro-zones enriched in antimony and arsenic;
  • uneven distribution of lead in intercrystalline regions;
  • the preservation of localised ore clusters.

Modern alloys show the opposite pattern — a high degree of homogenisation and the absence of stable trace-element gradients.

2.6.3 The Structure of Corrosion Layers and Their Relationship to the Alloy

The patina of ancient bronzes shows a close chemical and structural relationship to the underlying alloy. Microscopic analysis reveals:

  • zones enriched in tin and lead within the surface layers;
  • inclusions of copper oxides and sulphates containing impurity elements;
  • localised fixation of antimony and arsenic in corrosion products;
  • a layered structure with transitional mineralisation zones.

The key criterion is the continuity of the transition between the metallic matrix and the patina, reflecting a long, natural process of oxidation.

2.6.4 The Morphology of Secondary Patina Minerals

Within TETA, particular importance is given to analysing the morphology of corrosion formations. Ancient bronzes are characterised by:

  • dense crystalline aggregates of copper oxides;
  • micro-layered structures with alternating phases;
  • inclusions of complex copper, tin, and lead minerals;
  • heterogeneous zones of recrystallisation.

Artificial patinas, by contrast, are characterised by a superficial overlay of phases without deep structural integration.

2.6.5 The Criterion of Structural Consistency in the "Alloy — Patina" System

The most important diagnostic criterion of TETA is the presence of structural consistency between the composition of the alloy and the composition of the patina. Ancient bronzes show:

  • coincidence of trace-element associations in the metal and in the patina;
  • transition of elements from the matrix into the corrosion layers;
  • preservation of ore signatures in the oxidation products;
  • spatial correlation of enrichment zones.

In modern imitations, this consistency is absent: the patina forms as an external coating unrelated to the material's internal geochemistry.

2.6.6 The Diagnostic Significance of TETA Criteria

The set of microanalytical features used in TETA allows an ancient bronze to be treated as a single, evolving geochemical system. Diagnosis is based on the following principles:

  • priority of associative analysis over absolute concentrations;
  • accounting for the spatial organisation of trace elements;
  • analysis of the relationship between metal and patina as a single system;
  • identification of ore signatures as stable genetic markers.

Thus, TETA criteria provide a reliable means of distinguishing ancient bronzes formed from natural ore systems from modern items produced from refined metals with artificially created surfaces.

2.7 Practical Protocol for Applying TETA in Laboratory and Museum Diagnostics

TETA is implemented as a sequential system of non-destructive and microanalytical investigation of ancient Chinese bronzes. Its practical application is based on combining visual microscopic analysis, localised elemental mapping, and the interpretation of correlations between the alloy and its corrosion products.

2.7.1 General Scheme of the Analytical Study

The application of TETA involves several sequential stages:

1. Macroscopic assessmentRecording the overall condition of the surface, the character of the patina, and the corrosion zones.
2. Microscopic examinationStudying the structure of the patina, identifying zones of heterogeneity and transitional areas.
3. Selection of analysis zonesAreas with well-developed patina, exposed metal, transitional corrosion layers, or visible inclusions and defects.
4. Microanalytical studySEM-EDS, X-ray fluorescence analysis, or other methods of localised elemental analysis.

2.7.2 Principles for Selecting Analytical Zones

A key stage of TETA is the correct selection of analysis zones, since it is precisely the spatial heterogeneity of the "alloy — patina" system that carries the principal diagnostic information. Priority is given to:

  • metal/patina boundaries;
  • cracks in the corrosion layer;
  • areas of multi-layered patina;
  • localised inclusions of secondary minerals;
  • zones with visible colour and textural differentiation.

It is not admissible to limit the study to homogeneous surface areas alone, as these do not reflect the object's geochemical structure.

2.7.3 Microanalytical Interpretation of Data

When interpreting analytical results, primary attention is given not to the absolute values of elements, but to their systemic interrelationships. Within TETA, the following are analysed:

  • correlations of Cu–Sn–Pb as the basic metallurgical system;
  • the distribution of As and Sb as indicators of ore origin;
  • localised concentrations of Fe, Ni, and Co as trace markers;
  • the coincidence of trace-element profiles in the metal and in the patina.

Particular importance is given to identifying continuous transitional zones, in which the gradual migration of elements from the metallic matrix into the corrosion layers is observed.

2.7.4 Interpreting the Patina as a Geochemical System

In TETA, the patina is treated not as an external coating, but as an integral part of the alloy's evolution. Interpretation takes into account:

  • the layering of corrosion products;
  • the degree to which alloy elements are incorporated into mineralogical phases;
  • the presence of relict ore signatures;
  • the structural relationship between microrelief and chemical composition.

Particular attention is paid to signs of deep-seated patina formation, which cannot be reproduced by superficial chemical treatments.

2.7.5 Reliability Criteria for Results

Results obtained within TETA are considered reliable when the following conditions are met:

  • the presence of consistent trace-element associations in the metal and in the patina;
  • the identification of gradient transitions in element concentrations;
  • the fixation of ore-derived trace-element "tails";
  • structural heterogeneity consistent with natural corrosion processes;
  • the absence of signs of superficially applied artificial patina.

Failure to meet even one of these systemic criteria requires the interpretation of results to be reconsidered.

2.7.6 The Place of TETA in Comprehensive Expert Examination

TETA does not replace traditional analytical methods but supplements them, forming an integrated approach to the diagnosis of ancient bronzes. In comprehensive expert examination, TETA is used together with:

  • metallographic structural analysis;
  • X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy;
  • isotopic studies (where available);
  • microscopic analysis of the patina.

This multi-level approach allows an ancient bronze to be treated as a complex geochemical system, encompassing the stages of ore formation, metallurgical processing, and long-term post-depositional transformation.

Section conclusion

The practical implementation of TETA allows the transition from descriptive analysis of bronzes to a systemic geochemical reconstruction of their origin — forming the basis for a scientifically grounded distinction between ancient objects and modern imitations.

Section 3

The Unified Diagnostic Principle

Both methodologies treat the ancient artefact as a single system of "material — environment — time." Authenticity is determined not by external appearance, but by stable structural and geochemical changes formed over long historical periods.

Founding principle

Authenticity is determined not by external appearance, but by stable structural and geochemical traces of prolonged interaction between the material and its environment.

The integration of STA and TETA constitutes a new interdisciplinary field of scientific diagnostics, uniting microscopy, mineralogy, and geochemistry within a single approach to the expert examination of ancient Chinese artefacts.

Section 4

Fields of Application

STA and TETA together form the basis of a structured, interdisciplinary knowledge base applicable both to museum practice and expert authentication, and to fundamental research on diagnostic features by means of non-destructive analysis.

Museum practice

Scientific documentation of collection objects; non-destructive examination of museum-grade jade and bronze specimens.

Expert authentication

Objective structural and geochemical verification of objects presented for attribution; detection of later imitations.

Scientific research

Study of the burial-environment evolution of jade and bronze; comparative analysis across cultures and periods.

Digital archives

Formation of structured scientific knowledge bases for digital atlases of cultural heritage, building on this methodological foundation.
