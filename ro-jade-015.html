RO-JADE-015 · Late Zhou Ritual Jade

Ritual Lidded Censer with Stand in White Jade

White nephrite with even russet-brown patina, three-part vessel with pierced lid and stand, Shaanxi or Henan

White Nephrite (Russet Patina) Late Zhou, Warring States, c. 475–300 BCE Three-Part Lidded Censer Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a compound ritual jade vessel of the late Zhou period, composed of three functionally and plastically coordinated elements: a body, a lid, and a stand. Per the accompanying documentation, this tripartite construction is characteristic of the more elaborate ritual jade vessels associated with the Warring States period.

The body is rounded in form, with symmetrically placed zoomorphic handles carved in high relief. Per the documentation, the iconography of the handles allows more than one reading — stylised images of a predatory animal, possibly a tiger or a dragon-related creature — consistent with the symbolic vocabulary of the Warring States period. The lid is domed, with openwork ornament based on a stylised cloud-scroll motif, and is finished with a central bud-form knob. The stand is built with arched piercings and a stable looped base, reflecting, per the documentation, a considered engineering and compositional approach to the object as a whole.

Per the accompanying documentation, the combination of form and ornament allows the object to be read either as an incense vessel (xianglu, 香炉) or as a ceremonial ritual container, of a type associated with temple or aristocratic funerary practice; this page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense white nephrite of the tremolite-actinolite group, described as having weak translucency in thin sections and high mechanical durability.
  • Colour and structure: a transitional colour range from a milky-white base to saturated amber-russet tones, attributed to long-term secondary mineralisation involving iron compounds penetrating micro-cracks and porous zones.
  • Fabric: a compact, fine-fibrous structure with pronounced anisotropy; zones of secondary calcite and silica formation are noted at the base and lid, described as typical of prolonged residence in a burial or soil environment.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving with a high degree of detail, including fine openwork on the lid, complex plastic modelling of the zoomorphic handles, polishing that retains a soft silky-matte lustre, and precise fitting of the components without adhesives or mechanical fasteners — a technical level the documentation associates with developed late Zhou nephrite workshops.
  • Patina and wear: an even, diffuse russet-brown patina, most pronounced in the carved recesses, at the joins between components, and along micro-cracks and porous zones; micro-channels partially filled with iron oxides and secondary mineral deposits are described as consistent with natural archaeological ageing, with no indications of modern reworking or artificial patination noted.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported patina distribution — concentrated in carved recesses, component joins, and porous zones, with associated calcite and silica formation — is described in the documentation as consistent with a long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the compositional complexity of the vessel — its three-part construction, zoomorphic handles, and pierced cloud-scroll lid — situates it within the ceremonial repertoire of Warring States ritual jade, in which engineering sophistication and symbolic programme were understood as working together.

  • the zoomorphic handles as a reference to predatory-animal imagery associated with strength and status;
  • the pierced, cloud-scroll lid as an extension of Warring States decorative vocabulary into a functional, ventilated form;
  • the three-part construction as evidence, per the documentation, of a considered engineering approach suited to temple or aristocratic ceremonial use.

Taken together, per the documentation, the object is read as an example of developed late Zhou ritual jade craftsmanship, in which technical assembly and decorative programme combine within a single ceremonial object.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
