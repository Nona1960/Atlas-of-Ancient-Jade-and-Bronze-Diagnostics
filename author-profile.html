Biography

Professor Nona Dronova is a scientist working at the intersection of art history, materials science, and the traditional technologies of decorative and applied art. Her research focuses on the instrumental, microscopic, and visual-structural diagnostics of artistic heritage objects, with a particular emphasis on ancient Chinese porcelain, bronze, and jade across various dynastic periods.

Her first major international scientific undertaking was the creation of the Global Diagnostic Atlas of Ancient Chinese Ceramics. For this project, Professor Dronova developed an integrated methodological approach combining morphological analysis of form, examination of glaze microstructure, surface micro-relief, and the physical characteristics of technological firing.

Professor Nona Dronova is the Scientific Director and Founder of the Global Diagnostic Atlas of Ancient Chinese Ceramics. She defines the project's overall scientific concept, its methodological architecture, its standards of data interpretation, and the system of diagnostic criteria applied to every reference object in the collection.

The second stage of the project — the study of ancient jade and bronze — consists of building a structured, interdisciplinary knowledge base applicable both to museum practice and expert authentication, and to fundamental research on diagnostic features by means of non-destructive analysis.

The Integrated STA & TETA Methodological System

Under the scientific direction of Professor Nona Dronova, Doctor of Technical Sciences, an integrated methodological system for the non-destructive diagnostics of ancient artefacts has been established, based on the analysis of the long-term natural processes of their structural and geochemical evolution.

STA

Surface Transformation Analysis

Applied to the study of ancient jade, identifying the patterns of centuries-long mineralogical transformation of the surface layer formed under archaeological burial conditions.

TETA

Trace Element Tail Analysis

Used for the diagnostics of ancient Chinese bronzes, based on the analysis of trace-element "tails" of ore origin in the alloy and their relationship to the composition of secondary corrosion products (patina).

Both methodologies treat the ancient artefact as a single system of "material — environment — time," in which authenticity is determined not by external appearance but by stable structural and geochemical changes formed over long historical periods. The integration of STA and TETA constitutes a new interdisciplinary field of scientific diagnostics, uniting microscopy, mineralogy, and geochemistry within a single approach to the expert examination of ancient Chinese artefacts.
