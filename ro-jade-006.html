RO-JADE-006 · Neolithic Ritual Jade

Archaic Jade Figure of a Human Merging with a Totemic Creature

Greenish-grey nephrite, Hongshan cultural sphere, north-eastern China

Nephrite Hongshan Cultural Sphere SEM-Documented Patina Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a fully carved three-dimensional composition depicting an anthropomorphic figure in a seated posture, knees drawn up, shown in a state of symbolic fusion with a large zoomorphic creature. The zoomorphic component is distinguished by an elongated muzzle, pronounced bodily volume, and a schematic treatment of a mane-like feature, reinforcing the archaic, totemic character of the image. The composition is built as a single integral form, in which the boundary between human and animal is deliberately blurred, creating a visual model of transformation.

Through-drilled holes and asymmetrically placed perforations are present, most plausibly functional (for suspension or fixation within a ritual context) and, per the accompanying documentation, executed at an early stage of working. The piece is carved from a single block of nephrite using deep three-dimensional carving, sequential selective removal of material, and hand-drilling; final polishing was carried out after the principal structural elements had been formed, consistent with late Neolithic technological practice.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Scientific Examination — SEM Documentation

According to the accompanying documentation, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was carried out on this object (Institute of Steel and Alloys, Moscow), with the following observations reported:

  • Zones of secondary breakdown of the nephrite's silicate matrix, with formation of microcrystalline calcite (CaCO₃).
  • Inclusions of iron and manganese oxides, characteristic of prolonged geochemical transformation in a soil environment.
  • Natural surface micro-roughness ("dermatoglyphic microrelief"), reported as excluding modern mechanical reproduction.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination of silicate-matrix breakdown, calcite formation, and iron/manganese inclusion is consistent with a mineralised secondary surface layer formed over a long burial interval.

The SEM observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; the underlying SEM imagery itself has not been independently re-examined for this Atlas entry. They are presented as supporting evidence alongside the macroscopic description in Figs. 1–3, not as a standalone, independently re-verified laboratory result of this page.

Section 3

Cultural and Symbolic Context

Per the accompanying documentation, the composition is interpreted as a visualisation of a state of shamanic transformation, or of ritual union between a human being and a totemic creature. Comparable images are reported as characteristic of early cultures of north-eastern China, reflecting ideas about passage between worlds, the metamorphosis of the soul, and its protection through zoomorphic guardian spirits. On this reading, the figure may be regarded as a material embodiment of a mediating state between the human and natural worlds.

The documentation cites comparable material at:

  • The National Museum of China (Beijing) — late Neolithic anthropo-zoomorphic jade compositions.
  • The Liaoning Provincial Museum (Shenyang) — Hongshan-culture totemic figures with shamanic symbolism.
  • Archaeological complexes of north-eastern China — comparanda for the depiction of human-to-animal-spirit transformation.
This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date or function. The comparative museum references have not been independently verified for this Atlas entry.

Section 4

Condition

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Preservation: good, structural integrity with no indication of restoration.
  • Surface: multi-layered natural patina with calcified areas and mineral erosion.
  • Perforations: reported as original, with no evidence of secondary working.
  • Wear: even, consistent with prolonged archaeological burial.
