RO-JADE-007 · Western Zhou Ritual Jade

Ritual Figure of a Boar

Black nephrite with surface metamorphisation, Western Zhou dynasty, Weihe River basin

Black Nephrite (Actinolite-Type) Western Zhou Dynasty Weihe River Basin Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object belongs to the category of ritual jade sculpture of the early Chinese Bronze Age and is carved in the form of a stylised wild boar (or pig) — one of the recurring zoomorphic images of the totemic system of the Western Zhou period, per the accompanying documentation. The composition is marked by a generalised treatment of anatomical form: a massive body, an enlarged snout, a lowered head, and shortened limb proportions. The eyes are rendered as characteristic "cave-like ovals," consistent with early forms of symbolic reduction of a natural image.

Per the accompanying documentation, such images are interpreted as elements of a ritual-totemic system in which the boar could symbolise the power of the earth, fertility, or serve as a protective spiritual sign within a burial context.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Visual-Structural and Mineralogical Observations

The object is carved from dense dark nephrite, visually perceived as black, with local zones of secondary surface transformation. Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral composition and colour: the material is identified as actinolite-nephrite dominated by magnesium–iron silicates. Colouration is uneven — a saturated black base combined with brownish-ochre and locally lighter areas arising from secondary mineralisation. Microcrystalline inclusions of silica and phosphate compounds are noted, typical of prolonged residence in a burial environment.
  • Material structure: the nephrite shows a fine-fibrous metamorphic structure with pronounced anisotropy. In areas of light wear, a silky optical effect typical of high-quality nephrite from ancient sources is observed. Localised zones of surface degradation are linked to prolonged exposure to soil solutions and cyclical wetting–drying processes.
  • Working traces: under magnification, traces of traditional hand working are reported — linear scraping along the form-defining contours, localised polishing with quartz-abrasive systems, and smoothing in areas of plastic emphasis (head, back, lateral surfaces).
  • Surface patina and mineralisation: pronounced secondary mineralisation involving iron, silicon, and phosphorus compounds, most intense within relief depressions and microcrack zones. Localised yellowish staining is interpreted as resulting from prolonged contact with organic materials (burial textiles, lacquered inclusions, or other organic burial remains). The patina shows a natural gradient character, without indication of superficial artificial application.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, this combination — a fine-fibrous metamorphic structure with anisotropic optical effects, gradient secondary mineralisation concentrated in relief depressions and microcracks, and organic-contact staining — is consistent with prolonged burial rather than a superficially applied treatment.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural assessment, not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page.
