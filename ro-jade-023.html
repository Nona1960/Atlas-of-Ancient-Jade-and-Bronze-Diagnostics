RO-JADE-023 · Late Han – Early Tang Ceremonial Jade

Imperial-Type Jade Seal with Dragon Finial

Dense white nephrite with zonal ochre-brown patina, cubic seal body with a seated dragon finial and four-character inscription, Northern China

White Nephrite (Zonal Patina) Late Han – Early Tang, c. 1st–7th c. CE Seal with Dragon Finial Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a monumental square jade seal surmounted by a massive finial in the form of a guardian dragon, shown in a seated, stable pose. Per the accompanying documentation, the seal body is a strict cubic block with carefully finished faces, a form the documentation associates with administrative seals of high rank.

Per the documentation, the finial dragon has a flattened muzzle, powerful legs, and a tensed body plasticity read as symbolising authority, celestial order, and protection. The seal face carries a deeply carved four-character inscription in zhuanshu (篆书) seal script, executed, per the documentation, with a high degree of calligraphic precision. One character is noted as showing slight deformation, which the documentation reads as resulting from long-term mineralogical processes and micro-plastic surface transformation; this page notes that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an independently confirmed cause.

Per the accompanying documentation, the four-character inscription is tentatively read as 「奉命之寶」 (Fèng mìng zhī bǎo, roughly "Seal of the Mandate of Authority"), or alternatively as a related administrative title formula. The documentation itself states that a precise decipherment would require high-resolution imaging and spectral surface analysis; this page presents the reading as preliminary and unconfirmed, following the documentation's own qualification.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense nephrite of the tremolite series, described as a high-quality Hetian-type material.
  • Colour and patina: a multi-layered, ochre-brown patina with pronounced zonal variation.
  • Microstructure: calcite and siliceous incrustations noted as penetrating cracks and the carved inscription.
  • Manufacturing technology: deep hand carving executed in successive stages, hand polishing with varying stroke direction; no indications of mechanised or laser working are noted in the documentation.
  • Wear and ageing: micro-cracks, surface mineralisation, and natural leaching zones, read in the documentation as consistent with long-term ageing.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — zonal multi-layered patina, calcite/silica incrustation within the carved inscription, and natural leaching — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. The documentation's own summary statement that an accompanying "expert opinion No. HAN-TANG-SEAL-12" confirms the object's authenticity and exceptional museum significance is the documentation's claim; this page does not adopt it and does not itself offer an authentication verdict. It records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, seals of this scale and quality belong to the highest tier of early Chinese state symbolism. The dragon finial is read as a symbol of imperial authority, cosmic order, and legitimacy of rule; the cubic form is read as a marker of administrative stability and institutional power; and the four-character inscription is read as probably representing a formula of legitimation or an official title.

  • the object's possible function as a high-ranking state seal, per the documentation's reading;
  • an alternative reading, also proposed in the documentation, as a symbolic funerary regalia object associated with elite burial;
  • the dragon finial and cubic seal form together as markers of the fusion of administrative authority and sacred symbolism characteristic of early imperial Chinese ideology.

Taken together, per the documentation, the object is read as a rare example of monumental jade seal-carving reflecting the intersection of administrative power, sacred symbolism, and imperial ideology in early China. The documentation's own scholarly-attribution notes point to comparable seals reported in major museum collections; this page does not reproduce that comparative material, which belongs to a separate publications/bibliography resource rather than to the object record itself.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
