Chapter 1

General information about jade and jadeite

What are the attractions of antique jade stone-cutting products

Ancient stone-cutting jade products are distinguished by unique artistic and decorative qualities. They are one of the most popular trends in collecting decorative and applied artworks, determined by the traditional aesthetic preferences of the large population of Southeast Asia and, above all, China.

What you need to know about jade as a gemstone

Currently, jade is a rock consisting of the finest fibers of tremolite-actinolite amphibole with a characteristic tangled fibrous structure. The unusual strength of jade, which reaches 12,000 kg/ cm2 with uniaxial compression, is due to the peculiarities of its design. Its specific gravity is 2.8 - 3.1 g /cm3, Mohs hardness is 5.0-6.5.

Jewelry and ornamental jade is a dense, viscous cryptocrystalline aggregate of ordinary minerals of the hornblende group - light tremolite and green actinolite composition with the general formula Ca2(Mg, Fe)5[Si4O11]2(OH)2.

This is a stone characterized by high strength, various colors, transparency.

According to the texture, jade is divided into three groups: homogeneous, spotted, and spotted-interspersed. The composition of all three groups includes decorative varieties that differ in color and tone.

Features of the structure of jade

Jade has a unique property that distinguishes it from all stones. Jade is a "stone felt", a tangled fibrous mass of the finest crystals, giving it great viscosity and strength. They consist of thin tremolite fibers collected in bundles, which, closely intertwined, form a tangled fibrous aggregate. Each individual bundle consists of 10-30 sub-parallel fibers ranging in size from 0.005 x 0.01 to 0.01 x 0.2 mm. In nephrite, fibroblast structure, tangled-fibrous), tangled-parallel-fibrous, porphyroblast sheaf with fibroblast underlying tissue are most common.

Tremolite, actinolite, and hornblende are clearly established in various colored jade as the main components. The predominance of one of them, the different degrees of crystallization, and the development of other minor minerals determine the multi-coloration and variety of decorative features of jade with similar technological properties. In Eastern Siberia, green nephrites are mined -- from dark green to light apple-green, white, gray, honey, green-brown (marsh), and black. The most common are poly mineral spot-colored nephrite of green tones. Less common are homogeneous nephrite of mono- and mineral composition.

Green jade is a piece of high-grade jewelry and ornamental material. It has brightly colored green tones -- from light to dark green - and shines through in blanks up to 1.5 cm thick. Chinese stone cutters among green jade distinguish leafy-green, salad-green, apple-green, spinach-green. Green nephrites have a microfibre structure; inclusions of an ore mineral with a size of 2-3 mm are clearly distinguished in them, the content of which sometimes reaches 15%. A small number of such inclusions do not reduce the stone's artistic and decorative qualities.

In green jade, tremolite, actinolite and hornblende play an essential role. In the "swamp" and "tobacco" varieties, chloride and flake-like iron hydroxides are present in significant quantities.

The color of black jade is due to fine graphite. Frequently occurring white and whitish spots can be formed in some cases by tremolite, in others by talc or opal. An essential role in the composition of jade is played by an ore mineral (magnetite, chrome spine-like), which forms micro-and macro-inclusions and determines the development of speckled-interspersed varieties.

White jade has a snow-white color, more often with a faint bluish, yellowish, greenish, or grayish tint. It is characterized by the most uniform and fine-fiber structure compared to other varieties of stone.

Small-spotted jade is a low-grade ornamental material with dark, white, light gray, bright green spots.

In most cases, the group of homogeneous nephrites is characterized by uniform color and high transparency. Macroscopically homogeneous nephrites are white, green of different shades, gray, black, honey.

The trade name "tobacco jade" is a speckled, greyish-greenish-brown variety of jade. In plates, up to 1 cm thick, jade shines through. Then the unevenness of color due to the accumulation of a particular mineral is clearly visible.

Yellow jade is an almost mono-mineral rock with a characteristic fibroblastic structure caused by the development of elongated prisms of crystals (usual amphibole) forming a tangled fibrous mass with a thin-fibrous system.

Honey jade has a yellowish-brown color of various densities that shines through in plates up to 1 cm thick. It reveals an almost homogeneous structure, only in some parts, there is a weak intensity of the zoning of the color.

The microstructure of yellow and honey jade is needle crystals and their accretions. These are complexes of hundreds and thousands of elementary needle crystals connected together, having a diameter of about 0.1-0.5 microns. In honey jade, along individual microcracks and grain boundaries of needle crystals, the thinnest iron hydroxide films are developed, which causes a yellow color.

The black color of jade is due to the development of finely dispersed pulverized graphite, emphasizing the stone's finely wavy, feathery, complex structure. Uniformly colored black jade is rare in nature. Macroscopically, it is a massive black rock that accepts polishing well.

How do polycarbonate and apohyperbasite nephrites differ from each other

Apocarbonate and apohyperbasite nephrites are found in nature. All of them are composed of tremolite and discuss in the content of accessory and secondary minerals.

The distinctive features of polycarbonate jade are scarce light colors (from salad and olive green, light blue to white) and the complete absence of dark and colored minerals.

Apocarbonate nephrites are characterized by greater magnesia, fluorinity, and less ferruginous. They contain more lithium, beryllium, and cesium than apohyperbasite.

Apocarbonate nephrites are much less common in nature than the aposerpentinite industrial-genetic type). Their deposits are currently known only in China in the foothills of the Kun-Lun ridge, in Australia on the Eyre Peninsula, and in Russia in the basin of the middle reaches of the Vitim River.

In polycarbonate nephrites, the set of mineral inclusions is poorer. Diopside, apatite, zoisite, calcite, talc are found in them. The concentration of iron in polycarbonate nephrites is 0.23-1.30%, in apohyperbasite - 3.11-8.56%.

How is jade formed in nature?

Geologists consider jade formation a typical contact mineral among heterogeneous rocks during their chemical reaction with ubiquitous aqueous solutions. Sometimes nephrite occurs in serpentinites when olivine-pyroxene deep stones change. But the best in quality light gray jade grows on the border of magnesian marbles with granites.

Skarns are called the reactions between deep brines of different compositions occurring in other rocks. Under the action of alkaline solutions with an abundance of calcium and magnesium, soluble silica is released from granite, "gluing" alkaline earth elements into salts of silicic acids - amphiboles.

Apparently, a saturated solution and rapid crystallization are needed for amphiboles to settle in thin hairy crystals. Such conditions are rarely created. A space filled with a fluid with calcium, magnesium, and silicon is necessary for crystal growth. The optimal variant occurs when the xenoliths of marbles in granites dissolve in acidic granite fluids. The voids are filled with an alkaline solution. Jade masses appear in the holes due to the chemical reaction of magnesium, calcium, and silicon. But the conditions of crystallization must be unique.

Jade occurs at a 20-30 km depth at a 40-60 Kbar pressure. Solutions with abundant salts, supercritical, acidic, carbon dioxide, and fluorine (nephrites contain up to 0.6% fluorine). The peculiarity of jade is that it is enriched with light isotopes of water and grows in a solution enriched with light isotopes of oxygen. It is believed that the presence of light isotopes in jade indicates ancient buried rainwater, always rich in light isotopes. But the minerals of the depths are usually enriched with heavy isotopes of oxygen and hydrogen and are incredibly mobile. We think that the soft isotopic water of ancient rains is unlikely to retain its isotopic composition for billions of years.

Light isotopes always accumulate (fractionate) in the vapor-gas phase and then remain in the rain or snow. Fractionation is also possible in the superheated liquid of the earth's depths with retrograde boiling (steam), if zones of local low pressure appear. Then the gas (vapor) will arise at a depth of tens of kilometers. This is especially evident in clay rocks.

The authors, who found light isotopes of water in heavy minerals of crystalline shales, consider the strange isotopic composition of ancient minerals (corundum, hematite, ilmenite, etc.) related to the design of Archaean-Proterozoic rains. Professor A.M. Portnov believes that the main reason - the shrinkage of clay strata during their transformation into heavy minerals - creates local low-pressure zones with retrograde boiling and the concentration of light isotopes in heavy minerals arising among the steam saturated with mineralizers. Only retrograde boiling occurs when magnesia carbonates are dissolved. A similar phenomenon occurs during the formation of jade.

The paradoxical accumulation of light isotopes in jade makes it possible to explain its mysterious tangled fibrous microstructure. According to Portnov, jade formation is accompanied by a decrease in the volume of carbonate rocks when they are dissolved by acidic solutions in saturated silicon-carbonate vapor. Carbonates unstable in acidic granite fluid dissolve, creating a free space with reduced pressure and concentration of light isotopes.

Retrograde boiling of superheated magnesia-calcium-silicate solution creates a vapor-gas phase enriched with oxygen and hydrogen light isotopes, namely in the gas phase. This means that the growth of jade "hair" occurs in it. Growth conditions are created from a supersaturated mineralizer in such unusual conditions - a pair of hairy crystals crushed by pressure into a vicious stone "felt."

Where are the primary deposits of jade

Jade deposits are known in Canada, the USA, New Zealand, Australia, China, Poland, Germany, Italy, Mexico, Zimbabwe, Russia, Central Asia, and Southern Kazakhstan.

The indigenous deposits of jade, having a metasomatic origin, are divided into deposits in alpine-type hyperbasites (Ospinskoye, Ulan-Khodinskoye) and in dolomite marbles (Vitimskoye, Buromskoye). The chemical composition of hyperbasite nephrites is characterized by a high content of iron oxide (from 1.5% or more), chromium, nickel (by two orders of magnitude), and a lower fluorine content compared to dolomite marble nephrites.

The nephrites of the first group are dense, massive, sometimes shale, cryptocrystalline rocks of various green colors with distinct ore inclusiveness. Among the minerals of the first group, there are many varieties of substandard colors, the so-called "tobacco" (green-brown) jade. There are also black varieties of jade here.

In the jade associated with dolomite marbles, ore inclusions are practically absent. These are dense, massive rocks, translucent in areas, sometimes opaque-transparent, differing from jade from hyperbasites in lighter colors: apple-, light green-green, honey-yellow, yellowish-white, bluish. Such jades are an excellent material for the jewelry industry. However, superimposed oxidation processes often give soft pure tones brownish shades, which significantly reduces the quality of the stone.

The exceptional richness of the jade deposits of East Turkestan, located along the northern and southern slopes of the Kun-Lun ridge, was pointed out by A. E. Fersman.

For many centuries, jade, which came from East Turkestan, played an important role in China's cultural development. The main center of jade mining was the city of Khotan in East Turkestan. Up to five tons of jade were sent to the Chinese emperor annually from the indigenous deposits in the upper reaches of the Yarkand River in the Pamirs, until his son, who was sleeping on a bed of jade mined in Yarkand, fell ill. Then the emperor of China forbade breaking a green stone in the gorges of Yarkand. From then on, it was allowed to extract jade only from the river: enslaved people, standing waist-deep in water, had to intercept a stone rolling along the bottom and throw it ashore. Jade was searched for with bare feet, as the pebbles of this stone are so slippery that it is simply impossible to stand on them. According to the place of extraction, it was often called Khotan or Kunlun jade.

Where are the famous jade placers in China

The famous river placers on the northern slopes of the Kunlun ridge are of industrial importance. The main jade placers of China are located in the valleys of the Yarkand and Khotan rivers on the northern slopes of the bank. These deposits (together with the indigenous ones) are located within a strip stretched in the sublatitudinal direction by 600 km with a width of 100 km. Geologists think about the existence of a "jade belt" covering the northeastern coastal regions of the country (Liaoning and Hebei provinces), eastern (Shandong Peninsula), southeastern (Jiangsu and Zhejiang provinces), as well as the middle reaches of the Yangtze River (Hubei Province, Hunan). There are a series of deep faults of the earth's crust in these provinces, which penetrated carbonate sediments together with granites and raised the bowels in the form of a giant Kun-lun ridge.

Was there a Jade Way in the history of China

It was necessary to develop permanent trade relations to deliver an ever-increasing jade from East Turkestan. According to Professor Yang Bode, long before the opening of the Great Silk Road, there was a Jade Way that ran along approximately the same routes.

The Jade Way existed long before the Silk Road. Jade culture is an integral part of Chinese culture. Therefore, Khotan jade has long been famous in the Middle Kingdom; 10,000 years ago, people already knew Khotan jade.

It is said that Khotan jade, entering the central regions of China, necessarily passes through Yumenguang - Jade Gate. Therefore, even before the Han dynasty, the export of Khotan jade to the significant areas of China was already solid.

The main center of jade mining was the city of Khotan in East Turkestan, which supplied musk and jade in abundance. Here is how it is reported about him in an ancient legend: "The sacred river Yiu flows past the city from the peaks of Kuen or Kunlun and in their foothills, it divides into three streams: one is a stream of white you, the second is green, the third is black. Every year, when the fifth or sixth moon comes, the rivers overflow their banks and carry a lot from the peaks, which they collect after the water subsides. It is forbidden for people to approach the banks of the river until the Khotan ruler approaches himself to make his choice," writes the historian of Khotan Abel-Remyusa. He claims that jade is like the beauty of a girl, and if at the second moon an exceptional shine begins to creep from trees and grasses, it will mean that an eye has appeared in the river. That's why the city of Hotan is nicknamed by the Chinese Iyu-chan.

Obviously, a new impetus in the development of the northern directions of the "Jade Way" can be given by the mineralogical study of samples from all these.

What is the difference between Turkestan jade?

Turkestan jade is noticeably different from Baikal jade in color. It has a grayish tint.

High-quality Khotan jade is considered white as fat, yellow as chestnut, red as a cock's comb, black as varnish. The best among them is deemed to be transparent as the fat of a lamb's tail.

In shape, jade in pebbles is considered the most precious - fragments of rocks with a diameter from 1 to 15 cm rolled to varying degrees. Larger rolled chunks are called boulders and boulders. The rolling of sharp-angled pieces of jade occurs due to their countless collisions with each other in the process of cruising along the bottom of the river.

From which jade deposits were jewelry items made in Russia before the revolution

Until the 1850s, jade was imported to Russia from China. At that time, the Peterhof lapidary factory purchased dark green jade for a thousand rubles per pood. The price doubled for a higher-quality stone.

In the mid-1850s, geologist G.M. Permikin discovered jade boulders in the Eastern Sayan, and then the first root deposit was found. Products made of this jade, made at the Peterhof lapidary factory, were shown at world exhibitions in London and Paris in 1862 and 1867.

In the USSR, especially in the post-war years, the Soyuzkvartssamotsvety HPE revealed new localities in the Eastern and Western Sayan, Buryatia, in the north of the Krasnoyarsk Territory and the Polar Urals. What color are the jades mined in Russia?

In the deposits of our country, jade is known to be predominantly green (Eastern Sayan), white with a smoky hue is less common, the color is usually uneven -cloudy, less often striped, spotted, monophonic. A peculiar pattern is revealed in polished plates, which depends on the difference in color intensity uneven distribution of inclusions of magnetite and chrome spinelides. Many stones are translucent. On their surface, there is a hardening caused by the oxidation of divalent iron to trivalent.

How jade is mined

Sometimes jade is extracted directly from the riverbed. To do this, the river is artificially sprung in some places so that its waters flow faster. Standing waist-deep in water, workers stir up the bottom soil with long poles and rakes. Clay-sand components of the ground, having a lower density, are carried away by the current of water, and heavier stones remain to lie on the bottom. Further enrichment of sands extracted from mines or from the river is carried out by washing. Workers fill unique baskets with loose rock containing minerals and shake them in washing pits filled with water.

What you need to know about Jadeite

The bright green color of the Burmese jadeite stone has caused a severe shift in the traditional market of stone-cutting products. In the late 1700s, China began importing jadeite from Burma (now Myanmar). From this time, Chinese craftsmen began to make sacrificial vessels, jewelry, and many other items from jadeite. Since then, imported imperial jade has become even more revered than the original Chinese jade.

Jadeite is characterized by transparency and clarity with a clearly visible glassy luster. Its hardness is between 6.0 and 7.0 on the Mohs scale. The colors of jadeite are more saturated compared to jade. They include green, white, red, black, and delicate lavender.

For a long time, jadeite was not distinguished by external signs from jade. A.E. Fersman wrote: "In this case, we encounter a rare phenomenon of remarkable similarity of two minerals of different chemical composition. Jadeite is monoclinic pyroxene, while jade is a monoclinic amphibole. Just like jade, jadeite has a cryptocrystalline fine-fibrous structure of microscopic fibrous crystals entangled with each other."

Jadeite is characterized by fine- and fine-grained sheaf ( paniculate ) structure. The granular form of jadeite is visible to the naked eye or viewed through a magnifying glass with a tenfold magnification to distinguish it from jade.

Today, almost the only supplier of jadeite to the world market in Myanmar, where the main deposits are concentrated. Their operation has been carried out for many hundreds of years and is now under the control of the state. Interest in jadeite increases from year to year; it finds sales in America, Canada, Indonesia, Spain, China, Japan, the Philippines, and enters the markets of Western Europe. The increased interest in jadeite is explained by the fact that it is an integral part of Chinese culture

How to distinguish jade from jadeite during visual inspection

The name "jade" is a collective term used to describe two separate minerals: jade and jadeite. In the market, both are called jade.

Jade has deeper historical associations, and jadeite is distinguished by more popular emerald-green colors.

From a mineralogical point of view, genuine jade and similar jadeite are silica compounds with magnesium in the first case and sodium in the second. Both stones have a greasy luster and a variety of colors - from the color of milk to gray-green, green, dark green, or bright green tones of spring greens. But here are a few main differences between jade and jadeite

• \* Hardness: jade: 6 - 6.5, jadeite: 6.5 - 7. Jade is slightly softer than jadeite, with a greasy sheen.

\* Specific gravity: Jade: - 3.02, jadeite: 3.3 - 3.5. Jade and jadeite can be easily distinguished from most imitations, usually lighter than these stones.

\* Color. Unlike jade, jadeite shows more color variations. It is characterized by yellow, lavender, pink and emerald green colors.

\* Rarity rating . The best jade is considered to be white and slightly gray, and the best jadeite is translucent emerald green jadeite from Myanmar, which is also known as "imperial jade" and is the most valuable and sought-after gemstone.

\* Chemical Characteristics: jadeite is a sodium and aluminum silicate, and jade is a calcium and magnesium silicate.

\* Fibrous structure. Jade has a weak intertwining fibrous structure, jadeite has a very dense granular structure.

Chapter 2

How to distinguish jade and jadeite from fakes

Diagnosis of nephrite and jadeites

The first assumption about the nature of the stone is based on finding out what kind of mineral it belongs to, whether it is jade or jadeite, quartz (chalcedony or colored quartz), serpentinite, etc. This can quickly be done by studying its color, gloss, and general appearance. Still, you can be sure of the correctness of the definition only as a result of measuring one or another optical or physical constant.

Differences in the conditions of formation of minerals and synthesis of their analogs are reflected in some features of growth and internal structure. These features manifested in the zoning of crystals and the nature of the distribution of color and inclusions. The interior parts of the stones can be studied using a microscopic identification method.

The similarity of jade with some varieties of serpentine (jade) is so remarkable that unique methods have long been developed in the East to distinguish the actual from numerous fakes. So, in one of the lapidaries of the Middle Ages, dating back to about 1596, we read: "To find out if there is real jade in a stone, look at it by the light of a lamp; if you notice the light of the rising sun inside, then there is real jade in this stone."

What are the diagnostic properties of jewelry stones

Diagnostic properties of jewelry stones used during the gemological examination. These include transparency of jewelry stones, luster, color, refraction, birefringence, hardness, density.

Optical properties of jewelry stones

A preliminary conclusion about the name of the stone is made based on the results of visual diagnostics - to determine the transparency, gloss, and color.

Transparency

Transparency is understood as a solid body's ability to pass light rays through itself to one degree or another.

Transparency depends on the crystal structure, cracks in them, solid and gas-liquid inclusions. In fine-grained aggregates consisting of many tiny differently oriented particles, light is repeatedly refracted in different directions, scattered and reflected, as a result of which such sums are translucent or completely opaque compared to single crystals of the same mineral. For example, translucent or opaque chalcedony is a cryptocrystalline variety of transparent quartz. The transparency of jade and jadeite is determined visuall when viewing them on the lumen.

According to the degree of transparency, all jewelry stones divide into:

\* transparent - all colorless and slightly colored inserts, through the plates of which (thickness 3-5 mm) the object is visible (jadeite "imperial");

\*translucent or translucent - through which objects are not visible

•(jade white, gray, jadeite, opal, chrysoprase);

\*opaque (black jade, marble, serpentine).

Shine

Along with transparency, this is one of the most important diagnostic signs. The brilliance of a jeweler's stone is determined visually.

The shine is created by the light reflected from the surface of the standing stone; at the same time, its intensity, i.e., the amount of reflected light, is more significant, the sharper the difference between the speed of light in the air and this faceted stone, i.e., the intensity of the shine is more important, the greater the refractive index.

According to the nature of the gloss, the following types of it are distinguished: glass (emerald), greasy, or resinous (jade). At the same time, smooth and sticky luster belong to the same type; the term "greasy" is applied to light-colored minerals, "resinous" - to dark-colored.

Coloring

The stone's color in gemology is determined visually against a background of white paper.

Refraction of jewelry stones

The refractive index is an essential optical constant of jewelry stones. It is directly related to the phenomenon of the refraction of light.

Refractometers are widely used in gemological practice to determine the refractive indices of jewelry stones. The determination of refractive index on refractometers is based on the phenomenon of total internal reflection at the boundary of two media.

Density

Density is the ratio of the mass of a substance to the mass of the same volume of water. The density of jewelry stones does not directly affect their beauty. The density of precious stones ranges from 1 to 7. Stones with a density of up to 2 are light, from 2 to 4 are generally heavy, above 5 are heavy.

There are many methods for determining density: weight, volume, immersion, radiation, refractometric, analytical, X-ray, etc. To determine the density of jewelry stones, the most convenient, perhaps, is balancing in heavy immersion liquids as a method that does not require the destruction of the material and is quite rapid. The most applicable in this case are the SNVg bromoform (density 2.89 g/cm3, diluent - alcohol), Thule liquid - Hgl2 + 2KI (density 3.17 g/cm3, diluent - water) and Clerici liquid (CH2 COO 2T12 + NSOOT1 (density 4.27 g/cm3, diluent - water). When working, the densities of the determining mineral and the liquid, initial or diluted with the appropriate diluents, are compared. The exact density values are obtained by hydrostatic weighing.

Hardness

Hardness is the ability of a jeweler's stone to resist destruction. It determines its abrasion resistance during cutting, grinding, polishing, and operation. It depends on the nature of the rock and is determined by the Mohs scale. The mineral's hardness on this scale is expressed by the number of reference mineral that leaves a scratch. Most precious jewelry stones have a hardness above 7 (diamond - 10, ruby and sapphire - 9, topaz, spinel, alexandrite - 8, emerald, aquamarine, beryl - 7.5-8, garnet, tourmaline - 7-7.5, amethyst - 7, etc.)

Agalmatolite is an opaque decorative ornamental stone, a kind of pyrophyllite mineral. Various figurines and ornaments are cut from agalmatolite. White chalcedony is a white cryptocrystalline variety of silica.

Chalcedony contains 90-99% silica, with the highest content characteristic of light-colored and milky-white varieties. Chalcedony forms dense masses, leaky forms, nodules, crusts, fill voids.

In chalcedony, a more or less clearly distinguishable banding is visible, parallel to the outer surface of the formations or the walls of the execution cavity. This banding is not always visible to the naked eye. Still, it can be detected under a microscope in the sections oriented perpendicular to the outer surface of the clusters, or it can be seen by staining and etching.

The color of chalcedony is mostly uniform or only slightly varies from layer to layer. The color of chalcedony is always quite pale. Usually, there are stones of gray, grayish-blue, milky-blue, pale blue, grayish-green, greenish-blue, yellowish, or pale yellowish-brown shades, as well as grayish-white to light milky-white and almost colorless. Chalcedony of intense or dark bluish-green or yellow colors, as well as pinkish or reddish coloration, are much less common. The blue color of some chalcedony may disappear during calcination.

Chalcedony, as a rule, is translucent to transparent or cloudy; moreover, individual layers in one sample often differ in the degree of transparency.

The hardness of chalcedony varies somewhat; it is less than that of coarse-grained quartz, and is usually 6.5. The density of chalcedony is 2.57-2.64; fluctuations depend on the amount and composition of impurities, porosity, and water content.

Cleavage is absent. The fracture is uneven or cancellous. Chalcedony easily splits in the direction transverse to banding and parallel to the elongation of the fibers. At the same time, an unstable or splintery fracture with a reasonably well-defined waxy sheen is formed. It is possible to get a fracture and parallel layering.

Chalcedony is characterized by greater or lesser porosity, with isolated pores (or more often the thinnest tubes and filamentous voids) located parallel to the fibrousness. The porosity varies in layers or zones and is minimal in white or milky-white interlayers. Some stones stick to the tongue or quickly absorb water, sometimes with a faint hissing sound or with the formation of bubbles on a moistened surface. The porosity in chalcedony and its varieties, including agate, allows these stones to be artificially colored. Painted chalcedony is often sold under the trade name "Malaysia jade."

What tips can be given when buying jade products

A fake can be distinguished from jade by simple actions. There are generally accepted recommendations for a quick diagnosis of the stone among jade collectors. Here are some of them.

1\. Check for thermal conductivity. Even in a warm environment, jade feels cold to the touch of the hand. If you are unsure, it is recommended to hold a piece of jade in a closed palm until it heats up to about the same temperature as your body. Then put the jade aside for 30 seconds and touch it with the tip of your tongue. Genuine jade does not feel like a warm stone.

2\. Hardness testing. If the metal quickly leaves a scratch, it is most likely a softer mineral (usually a soft coil or agalmatolite) or some kind of polymer. It can be carried out from the inside with a blunt metal object in exceptional cases.

It must be remembered that bornite (a complex variety of serpentinite) sometimes "passes" a hardness test. Chalcedony will always pass the test, and there will be no scratches on it. Fortunately, chalcedony forgeries are not as common as those made from softer serpentinites. And, of course, genuine, archaic jades, whose surface layer has been changing for thousands of years, cannot be checked in this way.

3\. Checking translucency and searching for a fibrous structure. A visual inspection can reveal a lot. Jade is characterized by a tangled fibrous structure and barely noticeable venation. The translucent jade creates a specific visual effect, unlike most imitations.

4\. Check for roughness. To identify the features, it is recommended to hold the nail on the polished surface of the object. If it is made of glass, then the fingernail will slide over it and will not feel the most satisfactory roughness characteristic of the surface of genuine archaic jade. These sensations are explained by the fact that ancient jade was polished with soft abrasives; such a specific treatment shows a slight effect of "orange peel" on the surface of the stone-cutting object.

5\. The peculiarity of coloring. Although genuine jade comes in various colors, it always lacks the sickly yellow hue so characteristic of serpentinites.

6\. Check for the presence of musical ringing. Actual jade products produce a slightly more pleasant sound with resonance than objects made of plastic or glass.

How to distinguish genuine jade products from imitation products made of glass

Glass is a transparent, shiny, complex, and brittle substance formed by melting sand in a mixture with water or potash. There is no regular arrangement of atoms in the glass.

Imitations of white jade made of an opaque variety of Peking glass with a particularly dense structure, known as "Peking glass," are often sold on the market. They are often sold with deceptive names, such as "Henan jade."

The main diagnostic recommendation is to search for air bubbles using a magnifying glass of 10x magnification. Since glass imitations of jade contain air bubbles, it is recommended to look at the object in the light. The mirror shows itself under 10x magnification. Tiny swirls and bubbles are visible.

History of jadeite processing

From which deposits were jade extracted in ancient times

Two main types of geological sources of Chinese jade are known; sources associated with metamorphosed dolomite marbles and sources related to serpentinites.

Research involving scientific methods could solve issues related to the geological origin of jade in ancient China and the extraction of jade and its use. Analysis of early Chinese jade using X-ray fluorescence spectroscopy indicates that the geological sources of the material used for these ancient jade are most likely associated with dolomite marbles.

The geological sources of jade, which was used in the early cultures of China, are currently unknown to scientists. Such sources may have been exhausted in ancient times since jade can occur in small localized deposits.

Of particular interest is the content of iron and magnesium oxides, which are present in jade products used during the Neolithic period (Hongshan, Liangzhu, and Longshan cultures). Perhaps the geological sources for each culture were one or more related deposits. Unusually high iron content (0.35-17.95%) and magnesium (0.02-0.89%) were found in the ancient jade of the Longshan culture. This suggests that the source of jade is particularly rich in iron and manganese.

The jades used for carving during the reign of the Shang Dynasty and in Western Zhou have a wide range of compositions. Presumably, they came from different sources. Most of these early Chinese jade consists of massive fine-grained tremolite-actinolite.

In ancient times, jade was mined in the mountains by primitive methods of splitting fire and pouring cold water on hard rocks.

Professor Yan Boda, who devoted several exceptional works to studying the "culture of jade tools," suggested counting the "period of real jade" from the beginning of using Khotan mineral raw materials.

The scientist E. Schaefer also believed that Khotan, and later Yarkand, was the source of all the jade used in China. However, he did not wholly exclude the possibility of using local resources.

What scientific methods allow us to study the features of processing archaic jade

In China, more than 500 excavations of jade from various burial sites from the Neolithic to the Han Dynasty were analyzed at the Chinese Academy of Geological Sciences. In the last decade, such scientific methods as X-ray diffraction and IR-Fourier spectroscopy (IR-Fourier) have been used for these studies.

How long ago did they start mining and processing jade in China

The Chinese have been working with jade for more than 5,000 years, i.e., long before using metals. And some researchers believe that jade has been used in China to manufacture weapons, jewelry, and ritual items for about 7000 years ago.

The earliest jade objects were discovered by archaeologists in the middle and lower reaches of the Yangtze River. They were dated to the Neolithic era (4000-2000 BC).

Basically, these are objects of worship. They contain the earliest known examples of ritual objects. Archaeologists have found many ceremonial axes and knives, a variety of decorative circular and arc-shaped jade pendants, bracelets and necklaces, as well as several masks.

What particular subjects are found in the study of Sanxingdui culture

The most striking is the large bronze masks and heads with angular features, substantial almond-shaped eyes, faceted noses, and huge ears. Such features do not reflect the appearance of Asians. The three most enormous masks have the most supernatural features of all Sanxingdui artifacts: animal-like ears, monstrously protruding pupils, richly decorated torsos. The emperor's eyes imply powerful vision, large butterfly-type ears - powerful hearing. Most often, stone-cutting products of this culture are made of white Khotan jade.

As a result of radiocarbon analysis, the artifacts were dated to the XII - XI centuries BC. Ancient artifacts in Sanxingdui date back to the end of the second millennium BC - this is the period of the reign of the Shang Dynasty, which flourished in the Yellow River Valley in northern China, thousands of miles from Sichuan Province.

Most of the artifacts of the Sanxingdui culture are made of jade. They are distinguished by skillful carving.

The figures have bulging eyes and widely spaced ears. Some collectors and researchers consider the Sanxingdui culture derived from extraterrestrial civilizations.

The history of the discovery of the Sanxingdui culture

Sanxingdui is an ancient settlement located on the banks of the Yangtze River in the Chengdu Province. It belongs to the Late Neolithic and Early Bronze Age (2800-800 BC). The settlement is fenced with moats and earthen ramparts. The creators of this culture had developed bronze casting technology and built irrigation facilities.

In 1929, a local peasant dug a well and discovered a pit with jade products. By 1986, about 2,000 copper and jade products had been unearthed. Many Chinese archaeologists searched the area unsuccessfully until 1986, when workers accidentally unearthed pits containing thousands of artifacts. The items were smashed, burned, and then carefully buried. This event later led to the discovery of a mysterious ancient kingdom.

The sacrificial pits are believed to have been where the ancient Shu people sacrificed to Heaven, Earth, mountains, rivers, and other natural goods. Humanoid figures bronze animal masks with bulging eyes could be images of natural gods worshipped by the Shu people. The bronze figure of a standing man with a height of 2.62 m stands out - the largest and best-preserved in the world.

Sanxingdui covered an area of about three square kilometers. It was a large settlement for that time with developed agriculture, including winemaking. The production of ceramics, sacrificial tools, and mining was commonplace for him. According to archaeologists, for unknown reasons, the inhabitants suddenly left Sanxingdui around 1000 BC, and this mystical culture fell into decline.

Among the artifacts of Sanxingdui are gold and bronze masks-disguises and goldenrod with the image of human heads, which are obviously regalia of power. Many hundreds of samples of cast bronze products have also been found, including life-size and even larger statues of people and large-scale sculptural images of human heads with various headdresses (which indicates a stable social stratification). There were already associations of people in Sanxingdu that exceeded the tribe in the form of organization.

How are the features of the processing of ancient artifacts investigated in scientific laboratories

Silicone molds are removed from ancient samples to avoid any risk of damage to objects that will be examined under a microscope in a high vacuum. These molds are mounted on aluminum plugs and coated with a thin layer of gold to facilitate their examination using a secondary electron image in the high vacuum of an electron microscope chamber.

A thorough microscopic examination makes it possible to identify the features of grinding and polishing ancient jade artifacts.

The study of archaic jade studied by optical microscopy proved that a rigid rotary saw (a circular saw in which the center of the cutting disc moves around the circumference), pointed tools, tubular drills, and lathes were used to turn them.

What are the basic principles of jade carving

Since ancient times, the following sequence of technological operations has traditionally existed:

1\. With the help of intersecting cuts, I removed unnecessary material.

2\. In stripping, all the planes of the cuts were smoothed.

3\. The sculpture was gradually covered with rounded surfaces.

4\. The ornament was applied.

5\. The sculpture was polished.

The removal of excess material during carving was carried out with abrasives. A power saw removed the maximum amount of extra material during roughing.

Because carved products always have recesses, many rotating tools, abrasive heads, etc., were used to give them the final shape, with the help of which a surface of any curvature was created.

And the small size of these tools allowed us to obtain the necessary recesses and process them.

Having removed the maximum unnecessary material with coarse abrasive powder, they proceeded to process the product with fine-grained powder, revealing finer details.

The final stage of the manufacture of the carved product was polishing. Polishing was carried out on similar processes using polishing powders. The surfaces are leveled using wooden leather circles with free abrasive.

What stages of jade processing existed in ancient China

Stage 1. Pushing. This method was used to produce abrasive sand (powder). The sand could be black, red, yellow, depending on the composition of the crushed minerals. According to the color parameter, the type of sand was also set.

Stage 2. Preparation of the grinding solution. It was produced by stirring the base - abrasive powder with water.

Stage 3. Opening of the jade rock. The rock was cut with a jigsaw while a solution of black powder flowed into the resulting incision from the kettle. Due to friction, sawing was carried out. The rock itself was cut into plates.

Stage 4. Cutting. Jade plates were cut into cubes and rectangles with a sharp metal disk. The process was accompanied by simultaneous watering with a solution based on red jade powder.

Stage 5. Preliminary grinding. A steel rim (2-3 cm or 3-5 cm thick) and a solution based on red powder were used. At this stage, only the edges of jade blanks were polished.

Stage 6. Grinding. 6-7 types of steel discs were used for subsequent grinding, each with a thickness of 2-3 centimeters.

Stage 7. Removal of the inner part. Unique metal crowns were used to cut out the insides of the jade billet. The jade core was left simultaneously since its extraction was later entrusted only to the most experienced master. A special chisel was used for the procedure. If vessels with a narrow neck and a wide container were made, then unique crowns with a thickening at the end were used, then the inner part was expanded with a special abrasive.

Step 8. Drawing a pattern on the surface of the jade. It was carried out with the help of metal disks.

Stage 9. Carving. The internal openwork thread was applied with a metal drill by translational movements of the bow.

Stage 10. Drawing a pattern. Using a vertical or horizontal vise, the jade object was fixed, then the design applied in advance on the jade object was engraved with a bow with a stretched metal wire on it while watering with a sand solution.

Step 11. Drilling holes. The jade billet was fixed in a plank of the appropriate size and placed in a glass of water made of the bamboo-segment. With the forward movements of the bow, a metal drill was unwound, and holes were made.

Stage 12. Finishing grinding. Jade objects of considerable size were polished to a characteristic shine with a wooden disk, impregnated with an aqueous solution based on sand from topaz or other harder mineral rocks. Small objects were polished with a dried gourd soaked in a similar solution.

Step 13. Polishing was carried out on a polishing disc with skin glued on it, soaked in an aqueous solution based on topaz powder.

What traces of the tool on the surface of the product can be considered features of primitive technology characteristic of antiquity

When evaluating the processing features, it is necessary to pay attention to:

1\. Features of sawing. If the thickness of the flat plates is absolutely uniform, then this may indicate the use of modern diamond saws.

2\. How cleanly and accurately small details are processed, whether there is "clumsy negligence" in work. In ancient times, jade was a costly material, and the old master carefully processed every detail.

3\. In ancient times, careful grinding at a slow speed with soft abrasives created a "soft and satin" surface, which differs from modern polished surfaces with an intense gloss. It is necessary to study the subject carefully and find out whether there is evidence of the use of high-speed stone-cutting machines and diamond abrasives

4\. Features of drilled holes. This is a vital diagnostic sign. Each period of development of jade processing has its own drilling features.

What is the symbolic meaning of jade stone-cutting products?

Jade has a special meaning in Chinese culture. The Chinese call it the "sky stone" and believe that it symbolizes good luck, health, happiness and that it drives away evil spirits. What does the concept of "artifact" mean

The word "artifact" comes from the Latin artifact, i.e., artificially created. Initially, the term was used when talking about that phenomenon, property, factor, process, or object that could not be created or exist at all under normal, natural conditions. It implied a purposeful intervention or an unknown factor that led to the artifact's appearance.

Most often, we find this term applied to archaeological finds. It implies a man-made object that archaeologists study. However, today, this term usually means an ancient object created by man and an object of incomprehensible or unproven origin, sometimes even frankly not recognized by official science.

How does the study of the mineral composition of jade solve the problem of verifying the authenticity of ancient artifacts?

Over the past few decades, scientific analysis of early Chinese jade has been aimed at determining the content of their mineral composition and the characteristics of secondary surface changes. This is because physical and chemical changes in the surface of jade have been formed by nature for centuries and even millennia and cannot be made artificially.

Studying well-documented objects from archaeological excavations helps determine the artifact's origin by the analogy of indicators and dramatically contributes to verifying the authenticity of ancient jade objects.

What explains the selective red coloring of ancient jade artifacts

The increased sorption activity of the jade-tremolite surface is explained by the increased diffusion of iron hydroxides from surface waters by those surfaces subjected to sawing, grinding, and polishing. And the selective zonality of the distribution of hematite accumulations on the surface of jade is explained by the orientation of highly porous cross-sections of fibers that have a developed internal specific character.

Manganese and iron are permanent components of natural freshwaters, and their content often exceeds the levels of the main macronutrients. Soluble forms of manganese and organometallic compounds of iron present in surface waters are resistant to chemical oxidation by dissolved oxygen. The circulation of iron and manganese in reservoirs is that their compounds flow from the catchment area to an ancient burial site, where they are oxidized and deposited on artifacts. The migration of iron and manganese in surface waters largely depends on the activity of microorganisms. Biological transformation of both manganese and iron can be carried out as a result of Physico-chemical processes and the participation of oxidizing microorganisms.

What secondary changes of stone-cutting products can be proof of the antiquity of jade

It has been scientifically proven that changes in jade during weathering can produce different effects depending on the volume and chemistry of any liquid or gases that interact with jade.

Verification of the authenticity of jade is based on the study of specific physical and chemical characteristics of surface changes. It is assumed that certain modifications in archaic jade artifacts can provide primary evidence for diagnosing the antiquity of jades buried in the soil for long periods and distinguishing them from those manufactured recently.

Such changes include:

1. The natural increase of minerals on the surface of artifacts (epitaxy) (Additive crystal planes). It occurs under the condition that the total energy of the boundary consisting of the substrate-crystal, crystal-medium, and substrate-medium sections is minimal. Epipactis accretions actually occur when one mineral grows on the surface of the jade. If one crystal forms growths, then such formations are commonly called endotoxic. Visually, the pattern of increase is observed due to the coincidence or proximity of certain planes in the crystal lattices of minerals. Crystals rise above the plane and grow from the center in different directions while being characterized by a natural and correct location.

2. Internal cleavage (Cleaving veins). Internal cracks are caused by volumetric mechanical stresses of underground processes (high pressure, temperature, plastic deformations). Internal cracks are different from external cracks that come to the surface.

3. Selective weathering (Differential weathering). This phenomenon is associated with the difference in the degree of change in color, hardness, density, and recrystallization of the surface layer of the jade artifact under the influence of moisture, heat, and chemicals during prolonged stay underground. Sometimes channels and traces that appeared as a result of the activity of underground plant roots are marked on jade artifacts. Such media and trails can also be caused by the interaction of the plant roots surrounding the jade.

4. Corrosion of the polished surface (Dissolved pits). The corroded pitted surface has a shape showing natural unevenness and is formed by natural weathering. The body of mineral crystals cannot be imitated by artificial methods.

5. Surface swelling or the formation of raised crystals on the surface of carved artifacts (Raised relief). Recognition of the naturalness of the elevation can serve as a tool for verifying the authenticity of jade artifacts.

Increased relief occurs on the surface of jade artifacts both on small (up to 1.5 mm) and large areas, which may consist entirely of fibrous growths of the altered surface of the jade. The mechanism by which an increased relief is formed on jade artifacts has been determined. It is associated with a change in tremolite (and/or pyroxene) during the secondary growth of water-containing minerals, such as clays and/or micas. In some cases, the crystallization force is sufficient to lift crystals or jade fibers above the surface. This effect is present only on artifacts made before the beginning of the Song Dynasty.

It should be borne in mind that artificially raised relief can be made by cutting and grinding with further micro-sandblasting. Such works leave higher individual crystals with an artificial surface, which are usually polished and unnatural. But such artificial aging of the character is easily detected by an ordinary binocular microscope and a magnifying glass. In some cases, it is even visible to the naked eye. To create an increased relief, gluing drops of epoxy resin on the product's surface can also be used.

6. Pseudomorphoses of mica by jade (Exposed crystal plane (mica). This is the process of hydration and formation of sheet silicate minerals (clays and micas) associated with prolonged exposure to jade artifacts of groundwater.

Tremolite is replaced by talc in specific crystallographic directions, sometimes to the formation of complete pseudomorphoses; less often by antigorite or chrysotile; an aggregate of calcite, mica, and quartz; scapolite. In ancient products, the replacement of jade with tabular tremolite was noted. Replacement of tremolite with nontronite was found in hypergenesis zone.

Recrystallization entails an increase in volume or the appearance of irregularities on the surface due to secondary changes in individual crystals and large character areas. Since the growth of silicate minerals, such as clay and talc, is associated with the rearrangement of amphiboles and pyroxenes and requires a lot of time and observations of calcination zones on the surface is proof of the authenticity of the artifact.

7. Signs of wear (Determination marking(cow's hair)). This effect on jade artifacts resembles colored veins and cracks coming to the surface, resembling cow hair. Sometimes they are called "senile spots," "ant leg," or "cow hair ."Their formation is associated with the growth of salt crystals formed from waters that fall into capillary cracks in solutions. The salts contained in the solutions crystallize. As a result, pressure increases, capillary cracks expand, which contributes to the violation of the solidity of jade. These physical or chemical transformations can be caused by underground heat, pressure, and chemical reactions over millennia.

8. Traces of burial (Underground determination). Surface leaching is characterized by areas of change that are softer than the whole areas of the treated surface of the jade. This type of change is a selective dissolution or leaching along the boundaries of mineral grains at the microscopic level with highly acidic solutions (pH \> 9) and not a mineralogical change. Such an environment with a high pH can occur during the corpse's decay with which the nephrites were buried.

Bacteria begin destructive work on the surface of ancient jade, which prepares the ground for the appearance of microflora. What is bronze

Bronzes are alloys of copper with tin in various weight ratios but with a predominance of copper.

What is an ancient patina?

Products made of copper and its alloys are eventually covered with a protective film called patina. The patina can serve as an indicator of how ancient this product is. When identifying signs of antiquity, it is necessary to distinguish the types of corrosion (patina) on the product under study.

The fact that ancient bronzes are subjected to the destructive effects of corrosion for a long time (0.3 -- 2.5 thousand years) is proved by archaeological finds of bronze products of the Shang and Zhou era in China.

The color of the patina strongly depends not only on the components of the original alloy but also on the place where the product is located and on what conditions and how long it was stored. The shade of ancient patinas is most often greenish, but there are also other colors: blue, black, gray, brown, pink, red. Sometimes a combination of several shades can be observed on one product.

The patina can be matte and glossy. Its thickness can vary, especially a thin uniform layer that repeats the relief of the product is appreciated.

The conditions of metal smelting significantly affect the patina.

The composition and conditions of patina formation on copper and copper alloys determine the peculiarity of the technology and the period of the manufacture of objects.

The composition of patina is copper oxides. The most common are malachite and copper azure. The patina of bronze products also contains tin oxides. Accordingly, if some other metals are present in the initial alloy, their oxides will also be part of the patina. In its central position, the patina of bronze objects consists of the same salts but with tin compounds; if the bronze also contains lead and zinc, then the patina also includes lead and zinc salts.

The formation of different types of patina depends on the following factors:

\* Qualitative and quantitative composition of alloys;

\* the method of making the alloy, the thoroughness of mixing the components during fusion;

\* Small or large crystallization of metal;

• location and conditions in which the item was located;

\* the time during which the artifact was exposed to any influences.

How corrosion is formed on ancient bronzes

Patina is the result of the corrosion of a copper alloy. Corrosion is the destruction of copper alloys caused by chemical and electrochemical processes developing on the surface of an object during its interaction with the external environment. As a result of corrosion, metals turn into stable compounds - oxides or salts, in which they are found in nature.

The environment where the metal is rusted (corrodes) is called a corrosive or aggressive environment.

It is known that most metals (except silver, copper, and gold) occur in nature in the ionic state: oxides, sulfides, carbonates, etc., usually called metal ores. The ionic condition is characterized by low internal energy. This is noticeable when metals are obtained from ores. This is also characteristic of corrosion. The absorbed energy during the metal reduction from compounds indicates that the free metal has higher power than the metal compound. This leads to the metal in contact with a corrosive environment tends to move to an energetically favorable state with a smaller energy reserve. We can say that the root cause of corrosion is the thermodynamic instability of a system consisting of metal and components of the surrounding (corrosive) environment. The measure of thermodynamic instability is the free energy released when the metal interacts with these components.

In some cases, the adsorption, or phase, layers (films) that arise on the metal surface due to the corrosion process that has begun form such a dense and impenetrable barrier that corrosion stops or is very much inhibited. But the free energy itself does not yet determine the rate of the corrosion process, i.e., the value most important for assessing the corrosion resistance of a metal. Therefore, under operating conditions, a metal with a greater affinity with oxygen may turn out to be not less but more resistant (for example, the free energy of oxide formation in silicon or aluminum is higher than that of iron, and they often surpass iron in resistance).

Any corrosion process has several stages:

1) supply of a corrosive medium or its individual components to the metal surface;

2) interaction of the medium with metal;

3) full or partial removal of products from the metal surface (into the volume of liquid if the medium is liquid).

In the study of corrosion, we held views on the peculiarities of its development on copper and bronze products. These features depend on the interactions of cations and anions of corrosive compounds present in the soil or atmosphere.

The morphology of the malachite corrosion product was investigated on the surface of a Chinese bronze vessel of the Shang Dynasty period.

To understand which zones and why are subject to corrosion and patina formation, it is necessary to focus on some of the technological characteristics of ancient bronzes.

It is well known that bronze alloys harden in a specific temperature range, i.e., the crystallization process begins at one temperature and ends at another. At the same time, crystals also differ in composition: those that harden at the initial crystallization are richer in metal and have a higher melting point. Those set at the end of the process are enriched with a low-melting metal.

Trace impurities in the feedstock play a significant role in melting. Suppose the impurities are localized at the grain boundaries at specific points. In that case, these places can be the center of local corrosion damage.

How does a noble patina differ from an ignoble one?

Employees of the State Hermitage highlight the features of corrosion, which are designated as stable (noble) patinas. These patinas grow relatively slowly. When forming a noble malachite patina, a red or black patina of oxides, sulfides, and microparticles initially appears. Then it is gradually converted into a carbonate compound. This results in an enamel-like green layer. Because this transformation is very slow, such a layer conveys all the nuances of the object's surface. Noble patina does not cover the details of the thing and does not distort the shape. Noble patina grows in the atmosphere to about 80 microns, and then the growth rate decreases.

Noble patinas on bronzes usually have copper oxide - cuprite as the first layer (adjacent to the metal). The overlying layers are the leading carbon dioxide copper (malachite, azurite). Still, hydrated copper sulfate (broshantite) is found. Hydrated copper phosphates are usually found in objects from mass human burial sites or agricultural fields fertilized with phosphates for a long time. In tin bronzes, the patina also includes tin dioxide --- cassiterite. With a substantial admixture of lead in the metal, some lead oxides (Massicotte, occasionally plattnerite).

From the point of view of structural changes in bronzes, a solid solution of tin in copper is primarily subjected to corrosion. As long as the entire solution has not rusted, intermetalloids are poorly affected by pollution.

Unstable, rapidly developing (so-called "wild") patinas, as a rule, include copper chlorides (atacamite, paratacamite) and bromides. By the way, the presence of the latter is a perfect sign in the sense of authenticity since accomplices usually do not use bromine compounds for patination.

What layers of minerals make up ancient patinas

The surfaces of ancient bronzes are covered with a two-layer corrosion crust containing minerals of a greenish-blue color, from under which reddish-brown spots of copper cuprite protrude in some areas.

The patina contains many different minerals: quartz, gypsum, calcite, maghemite, butlerite, and various other compounds. Sometimes alloys break down into their constituent elements: copper, silver, zinc. These metals (in their pure form) can be concentrated both inside the object and on the surface. In addition, the patina contains oxides and hydroxides, such as cuprite, lead oxide, zinc oxide. Carbonates are present in the patina.

The enamel-like green patina layer is a stable and slow-growing pseudomorphosis. At the same time, the shape of the object does not change. Corrosion is distributed evenly over the surface without etching the boundaries of the alloy grains. As the first layer (adjacent to the metal), copper oxide --- cuprite is present in the patina. The overlying layers are formed by copper carbonate (malachite and azurite), but hydrated copper sulfate (brochantite) is also found.

Microscopic studies of corrosion products have revealed that noble patinas consist of three layers. The ratio of the thickness of the layers in different patinas may vary.

The inner layer is brown-red (smooth surface), tightly attached to the metal base. It consists of copper oxide (cuprite). This is the only natural protection of copper from oxidative processes.

The middle layer consists of copper salts, complex and straightforward. It is greenish-blue, more friable. It contains a surface modified with green copper compounds, rough pits, and lamellar thin and thick crusts. In some places, it has a glassy luster.

The outer layer is represented by light beige growths with inflows of irregular shape and different thicknesses, which easily crumble. The corrosion products of this layer contain copper sulfides and sulfates (brochantite and bonatite), halides (atacamite, paratacamite), phosphates (cornetite, superphosphate hydrate, libetenite, pseudomalachite, sampling).

What minerals are present in the patina of ancient bronzes

Several minerals are formed on ancient bronzes:

Kuprit. Copper oxide. It is painted in different shades: reddish-brown, purple, lead-gray, black, or cherry, sometimes with a diamond sheen.

Cuprite is opaque, hard, brittle, and insoluble in water. It is characterized by the formation of dense masses of fine-grained structure. The thickness of the cuprite can reach several millimeters. Cuprite often preserves essential details of the original surface. It is often riddled with cracks or has cavities filled with copper chloride.

Cassiterite. Tin dioxide. It does not form an independent layer. Located in the mass of cuprite. Includes small powdery accumulations in cracks and can be mixed with copper salts. On bronzes with a high tin content, cassiterite can form a fine-grained, smooth surface layer of a greenish-gray color.

Cerussite. Lead carbonate. Lead is located in bronze in the form of globules. If it is corrupted, then cuprite forms in its place. Forms a layer of greenish-gray color under the outer layer of malachite.

Malachite. The vessel's surface is located both in the upper and lower layers. The outer layer of corrosion products consists of essential salts of divalent copper. Malachite accumulations are localized in the upper parts of the cementation zone and develop as earthy differences and thin crusts. Under a microscope, malachite detects a cryptocrystalline, fine-grained, and fine-fibrous structure. The fibers are grouped into growth cones, radially radiant bundles, and sub-parallel aggregates. The usual forms of excretion are dense or powdery aggregates, deposits, primers, buds, spherocrystals. From dissolved minerals, pores and voids remain in the patina, which malachite does not always fill entirely and creates a bizarre nostril-like kidney-shaped surface. Different shades of green color of malachite depend on the inclusion of other minerals in it: chrysocolla, asperolite, elite, etc. For example, chrysocolla gives malachite a bluish tint.

How malachite is formed during corrosion of ancient bronzes

Most researchers consider malachite to be a typical mineral of the corrosion zone of antique copper and bronze objects.

In the patina, malachite is deposited due to the percolation of copper sulfate solutions and their reaction with carbonate rock particles and organic residues. Usually, malachite is accompanied by cuprite, chrysocolla, azurite, copper phosphates, and other oxidized copper minerals.

Azurite and malachite are often found together. Their banded accretions are called azuromalachite. Azurite is less stable and gradually turns green in humid air, turning into malachite. It even covers old bronze items that are found during archaeological excavations.

Usually, malachite crystals on ancient patinas are fragile - from hundredths to tenths of a millimeter, and they are up to 10 mm long, and only occasionally, under favorable conditions, massive multi-ton drips of dense matter can form, consisting of a mass of clumped crystals.

How to imitate patinas on bronze products

To diagnose signs of antiquity, the main task is to identify artificial patinas.

Artificial patina is created with the help of certain chemicals. Thousands of years ago, people learned to forge a patina. During the Renaissance, the patina was especially popular: it was applied to monuments and other cultural objects to create the effect of antiquity. For thousands of years, patination techniques have been applied to copper and bronze objects to achieve specific effects.

Special substances are applied to objects to create an artificial patina, usually containing some acids and oxidizers. This patina can be transparent or frosted. It helps to protect the metal from fading and corrosion.

Artificially induced green and blue patinas can be obtained in the following way. A greenish-blue two-layer (the first layer is copper oxide) patina can be brought in a solution of copper nitrate with a concentration of 200 g/l. The key is applied to the exhibit with a brush and left for 12-16 hours. After washing with cold water and drying, the surface is treated with a rag with a thin pumice powder and then patinated 3-4 more times. Gradually, a greenish-blue patina is formed with a solid, slightly translucent inner dark brown dense layer of copper oxide.

How to distinguish a noble ancient patina from a modern, quickly induced.

Valid for the research methodology was familiarity with scientific works on patina's study, the formation mechanism that indicates the duration of its history. The ancient ore consisted of secondary minerals of sulfide ore oxidation zones (mixtures of malachite, azurite, cerussite, smithsonite, antimony, and bismuth oxides). Accordingly, when diagnosing a patina, it is recommended not to perform an elemental analysis but to identify the composition of minerals included in the patina. The main diagnostic feature is the intensity, speed, and uniformity of penetration of corrosion processes deep into the metal. Since corrosion is a rather lengthy process, the methods of formation of natural patina are similar to the biological functions of forming minerals in the oxidation zones of sulfide-copper deposits.

Unlike artificially induced patinas, characterized by uneven penetration along grain boundaries, ancient patinas form slowly, and corrosion on old bronze products spreads evenly over the surface.

Why are traces of osmium found in the corrosion products of some ancient bronze vessels

Osmium belongs to the elements of the platinum group. It is refractory, heavy, and forms natural alloys with iridium and platinum. It may contain other aspects of the platinum group.

When studying the structure of one vessel, we drew attention to the heterogeneity of the bronze alloy and the presence of solid minerals containing rhodium and osmium in it. These stable minerals have a very high melting point; hence they got into the ore as a mechanical admixture of volatile oxide compounds of antimony and rare stable minerals of platinoids. The melting point of the ore decreased due to additives to the principal carbonate copper ore (malachite, azurite, etc.)

What is the methodology of scientific research of ancient bronzes?

Stylistic analysis visual and microscopic examination of corrosion products could not serve as a sufficient basis for the study of Chinese bronze. To diagnose the signs of antiquity, it was necessary to develop a methodology based on modern methods that make it possible to distinguish this product from several repetitions (copies, fakes) of modern times.

The developed guidelines can help attribute bronze objects in the museum and private collections but do not have an unambiguous attribution to determine the place and time of manufacture.

To work out the technique of non-destructive diagnostics of the antiquity of copper alloys, we studied a bronze object, presumably made in China during the reign of the Shang Dynasty (which is made in a typical style for this period has pronounced characteristics).

A preliminary scientific examination was carried out to attribute the bronze vessel. The object entered the private collection in 1910. Item Description: Wine vessel in the form of a deer with a lid (Ritual wine vessel, Shang Dynasty). The lid is decorated with a bird, the entire surface is decorated with characteristic relief ornaments. The safety is high, but the entire inner and outer surface is covered with a layer of corrosion of greenish, bluish, beige, red, and black colors. Sizes: height 35 cm, length 35 cm, width 15 cm.

The next task was to search for and compare this wine vessel with similar and related items from famous museum collections.

Considering that this item entered a private collection from random finds without proper archaeological contexts, a similar bronze vessel of the Shang era, which is in the collection of the Palace Museum in Taipei (Taiwan), served as an assumption and critical evidence to determine its age. During the external examination, the attribution of this item as an earlier one was carried out.

The hypothesis was based on the foundations of the study of typology proposed by archaeologist Pitt Rivers, which are based on the principles of gradual changes and according to which the more complex the product, the more cultural development, and progress are expressed in it, i.e., a more straightforward design implies greater antiquity.

By the recommendations of art historian Oscar Montelius on finding similarities and differences in the dating of bronze products, specialists of the Scientific Center for Certification and Evaluation during the preliminary scientific examination attributed this vessel as presumably made in Ancient China during the reign of the state of Shang-Yin, which existed from 1600 to 1027 BC to the north of the outlet of the Yellow River on the Great Chinese Plain.

Preliminary scientific hypotheses were based on modern studies of ancient Chinese metallurgy during the Shang Dynasty.

Analyzing the results of the latest scientific research in the field of archeology, the characteristics of materials, and the features of experimental modeling of ancient metallurgical processes, we came to the conclusion that the signs of antiquity include the complex composition of the bronze alloy, its heterogeneity, corrosion features, and unusual impurities.

It is well known that when studying ancient metal products, first of all, it is necessary to determine the most acceptable method of sampling to minimally damage the surface and ensure the safety of the artifact. It was also taken into account that the object has a variable composition of the ligature, so the chips and patina were removed from the vessel in different places.

Samples of alloy, patina, and patina destruction products were collected from the surface of the bronze vessel manually using a jewelry scraper (steel scraper). Conclusions of a scientific study to determine the signs of the antiquity of Chinese bronzes

Elemental analysis of the alloy revealed the presence of a complex composition from which this bronze vessel is made. The conducted studies confirmed that the object had an imperfect manufacturing technology; therefore, the alloy is inhomogeneous, multicomponent, and contains mechanical impurities.

Mechanical (non-molten, interspersed) impurities are tiny grains of minerals of rare elements belonging to the metals of the platinum group: platinum, osmium, and rhodium. The uneven distribution of copper, lead, tin, antimony is characteristic. Such a complex composition is a good sign of antiquity.

So, the signs of the antiquity of this vessel are

• \* the complex composition of the alloy and its heterogeneity;

\* local patina heterogeneity in terms of component content;

\* corrosion features, patina composition, and its heterogeneity;

\* The presence of secondary minerals in the patina: cuprite, malachite, azurite-brochantite;

\* The presence of areas with an increased concentration of impurities of rare elements.

Our data show that there were other ore deposits in the Shang era with a set of their own indicator elements. At the same time, we consider it possible to melt bronze not from primary refractory sulfide ores but from low-melting minerals of the weathering crust. Here, mixtures of copper, lead, zinc, antimony carbonates enriched with ore metals with an admixture of arsenates of the same metals and a mechanical admixture of platinoids in the composition of a fine fraction accumulate. It is known that osmium is also found in compounds with sulfur and arsenic: ehrlichmanite, osmium laurite, osarsite. These minerals are called sulfoarsenides. These minerals accumulate in oxidation zones, are easy to extract, and are characterized by low melting with metal melting at 700 - 800 0 C. The content of osmium in ores, as a rule, does not exceed 1x10-5.
