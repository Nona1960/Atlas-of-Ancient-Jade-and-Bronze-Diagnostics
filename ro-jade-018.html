RO-JADE-018 · Eastern Zhou Ritual Jade

Ritual Figure of a Curled Heron in White Jade

White nephrite with even reddish-brown patina, curled zoomorphic bird form, Yellow River basin

White Nephrite (Reddish-Brown Patina) Eastern Zhou, Warring States, c. 475–300 BCE Curled Bird Figure (Heron) Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a ritual jade figure interpreted, per the accompanying documentation, as a heron, worked in a stylised manner characteristic of the late Zhou period. The composition is built on a closed, stable form, with the neck curved and pressed tightly against the body.

The sculptural treatment is generalised, yet retains key anatomical markers: a curved beak, schematised but emphasised eyes, and a conventionalised indication of the wings. The wing surface is engraved with parallel lines imitating feather structure. Per the documentation, the overall plasticity of the object is characterised by an inward compactness and stillness, producing an effect of contemplative compositional closure.

Per the accompanying documentation, jade figures of this kind are read, within Warring States burial tradition, as symbolic entities connected with conceptions of the soul, its flight, and its passage between worlds. White nephrite, particularly of the Hetian type, is noted as traditionally associated with purity, sacred light, and moral perfection. This page presents these readings as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense tremolite-type nephrite with a homogeneous fibrous structure and weak natural translucency.
  • Colour and structure: a light creamy base with a warm yellow-ochre undertone, with zones of pronounced reddish-brown patination attributed to iron (Fe³⁺) compounds concentrated in the carved recesses and engraved zones.
  • Fabric: a dense fibrous organisation without through-cracks; microscopic grooves and delamination lines, partially infilled with secondary mineral deposits, are noted as consistent with long-term natural ageing of the material.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving with sequential shaping — roughing out the main body volume from a single block, fine engraving of the feather ornament, formation of key anatomical accents (beak, eyes, wings), and a soft finishing polish; no indications of mechanised working or modern abrasive technique are noted in the documentation.
  • Patina and wear: an even, diffuse reddish-brown patina, most pronounced in the deeper carved zones, along the feather-line engraving, and in micro-cracks and structural pores; the patina is described as having a natural gradient character, consistent with prolonged interaction between the nephrite and a soil-geochemical environment. No signs of artificial ageing or modern restoration are noted in the documentation.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported patina distribution — concentrated in the carved recesses, feather-line engraving, and structural pores, with a natural gradient character — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, jade bird figures of this type belong to the burial tradition of the Warring States period, in which the bird image was read as a symbolic mediator connected with conceptions of the soul, its flight, and its passage between worlds.

  • the closed, curled composition as an expression of stillness and contemplative compositional closure, per the documentation's reading;
  • the choice of white nephrite as carrying associations of purity, sacred light, and moral perfection within the broader symbolic vocabulary of jade;
  • the heron-form reading as one instance of a wider repertoire of avian imagery associated with mediation between the living and ancestral realms.

Taken together, per the documentation, the figure is read as an example of Warring States burial jade in which reduced, symbolically dense form and a stable bird iconography combine to express ideas connected with the soul's passage after death.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
