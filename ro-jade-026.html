Nephrite Pebble Carving: Budai with a Bat

Small figural carving worked directly from a river pebble, exploiting the stone’s natural skin and colour zonation (qiaose 巧色 technique)

Proposed attribution: Chinese folk nephrite carving, late Qing – early Republic tradition (stylistic placement)
Proposed region: China; pebble material of a type historically associated with Khotan (Hotan), Xinjiang
Material: nephrite (river-pebble form, retained natural skin)
Fig. 1 — Front face: Budai (seated, upper register) and the bat, carved from the pale zone of the stone.
Fig. 2 — Reverse: uncarved, retaining the pebble’s natural water-worn form and dark skin at one end.

01 Object Description and Attribution

The object is a small figural carving worked from a single oval river pebble of nephrite, retaining its natural water-worn form. The composition — a seated, smiling Budai (布袋, the folk deity of contentment, popularly conflated with Maitreya) in the upper register, and a bat (蝙蝠, biānfú) in the lower register — is carved in shallow-to-medium relief across the front face only; the reverse (Fig. 2) is left entirely in its natural, unworked pebble state, including a dark, oxidised patch at one end.

The subject is a well-established rebus pairing in Chinese folk decorative art: the bat (fú, 蝠) is a phonetic homophone for “good fortune” (fú, 福), and its combination with the genial figure of Budai is a standard auspicious motif meaning, in essence, “happiness and fortune arriving.” This pairing and the carving convention it belongs to are common in Chinese folk and export-adjacent nephrite carving of the late Qing and early twentieth century, though the type itself has no single fixed date range and was produced over a long period.

02 Mineralogical and GOM Diagnostic Analysis

2.1 Material and pebble morphology

The stone is an oval, smoothly water-worn pebble of grey-green nephrite with a darker, brownish-black zone concentrated at one end (Fig. 2). This dark zone is consistent with a natural “skin” (皮, pí) — the oxidised outer rind that forms on river-pebble (“seed”, 籽料 zǐliào) nephrite through long exposure to iron-bearing groundwater after the parent rock has broken down and been transported as alluvial pebbles. Retaining this skin deliberately, rather than grinding it away, is a recognised and long-established convention in Chinese pebble-jade working, used both to demonstrate the natural origin of the material and, where the carver incorporates it into the design, as a mark of skill.

2.2 Colour zonation and carving technique (qiaose)

The carver has used the stone’s natural two-tone banding compositionally: the darker grey-green body of the pebble forms the ground and the figure of Budai, while the bat and the drapery folds below are cut into the paler, cream-toned layer of the same stone (Fig. 1). This selective exploitation of a jade’s natural colour zones to compose a design is a specifically named and well-documented Chinese lapidary technique, qiaose (巧色, “skillful colour”), attested from as early as the Shang dynasty and developed extensively as a refined art in Qing-dynasty jade carving; it remains one of the most commonly used techniques in Chinese jade carving today. Its presence here, competently handled, is a genuine positive skill indicator for the piece, independent of the date question addressed below.

2.3 Surface condition

The carved surfaces show a matte finish with localised natural gloss on the raised planes of the relief, and softened, slightly worn definition in the recesses and at the edges of the composition — consistent with long handling and hand polishing. Confirming the absence of modern rotary-tool marks, as distinct from traditional abrasive hand-finishing, would require closer physical or low-power microscopic examination than is possible from the two images available; this is noted as a further diagnostic step rather than a settled conclusion.

03 Cultural Context

The bat-and-Budai rebus belongs to a broad family of Chinese decorative homophone symbolism in which visual motifs stand in for auspicious phrases through shared pronunciation — the pairing of a bat with a figure of abundance or contentment is one of the most common of these throughout late-Qing and Republican-period folk decorative arts, appearing across media from jade and ivory carving to textiles and porcelain. The choice to work directly from an intact pebble, retaining its natural skin and using its internal colour banding as compositional material rather than carving a fully finished, uniform block, situates this piece within the pebble-jade (zǐliào) carving tradition that prizes the visible link between the finished work and its natural stone origin — a connoisseurship value distinct from, and complementary to, the fineness of the carving itself.
