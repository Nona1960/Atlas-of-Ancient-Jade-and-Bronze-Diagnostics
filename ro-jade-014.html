RO-JADE-014 · Western Zhou Ritual Jade

Ritual Bird Figure in Brown Nephrite

Brown-green nephrite with epidote and serpentine inclusions, stylised bird form, Luo–Wei river basin, Shaanxi

Brown Nephrite (Epidote/Serpentine) Western Zhou, c. 11th–8th c. BCE Zoomorphic Bird Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a ritual jade figure carved in brown nephrite in a stylised bird form. Per the accompanying documentation, the iconography allows several readings — a symbolic heron, a falcon, or an early form of a phoenix-related being — and is characteristic of the early phase of Western Zhou artistic tradition.

The composition is defined by clearly articulated anatomical elements: a curved beak, a drop-shaped eye, and symmetrical incised feather ornament across the breast and folded wings. The lower body retains a constructive protrusion, read in the documentation as likely intended to secure the figure within a ritual assemblage or burial structure; this page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Stylistically, per the documentation, the figure reflects a transitional stage between the schematic forms of the Neolithic and the more naturalistic sculpture of the early Zhou period, a shift associated with increasing ritual differentiation and an elaborating symbolic system within burial practice, in which the bird image served as a mediator between worlds and a symbol of posthumous ascent.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: a mixed-type nephrite, described as a metamorphosed amphibolite-group rock dominated by actinolite fibres and partially affected by serpentinisation.
  • Colour and structure: a brown-green zonal colouration with areas of olive and ochre tone, attributed to natural inclusions of epidote, iron oxides, and talc-group phases formed during regional metamorphism and subsequent surface transformation.
  • Fabric: a massive, unevenly metamorphosed structure with a pronounced fibrous texture and a silky lustre retained in zones where the original crystalline orientation survives; localised zones of structural disorder are read as natural growth defects combined with later physico-chemical transformation.
  • Manufacturing technology: linear scraping along the direction of the feather ornament, local polishing with mineral abrasives, and relief-smoothing concentrated in symbolically prominent zones (head, breast, wings); the technical profile is described as consistent with quartz-feldspar abrasive methods associated with Western Zhou nephrite working.
  • Patina and ageing: patina distribution concentrated in the recessed carving, in areas of presumed contact with the burial environment, and along micro-cracks and fibre boundaries; micro-inclusions of secondary calcite and siliceous deposits are noted as consistent with prolonged interaction with groundwater and a burial-related geochemical environment.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported patina distribution — concentrated in recessed carving, presumed contact zones, and fibre boundaries, together with calcite and siliceous micro-inclusions — is described in the documentation as consistent with a multi-stage natural surface evolution rather than a surface-level artificial treatment. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the bird form belongs to a durable symbolic tradition of early China linked to conceptions of the soul, the passage between worlds, and ritual transformation. The specific iconography — read alternately as heron, falcon, or an early phoenix-related being — places the object within the broader repertoire of avian imagery associated with mediation between the living and ancestral realms.

  • the stylised bird form as a mediator figure between worlds, per the documentation's reading;
  • the symmetrical feather ornament as an expression of the increasing anatomical and symbolic elaboration of early Western Zhou ritual sculpture;
  • the basal constructive protrusion as a possible fixture point within a ritual or burial assemblage.

Taken together, per the documentation, the figure is read as reflecting the transition from schematic Neolithic zoomorphic forms toward the more naturalistic sculptural language of the early Zhou period, within a burial practice in which the bird image carried significance related to posthumous ascent.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
