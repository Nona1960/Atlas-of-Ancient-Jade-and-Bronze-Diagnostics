RO-JADE-019 · Late Zhou – Early Han Ritual Jade

Sculptural Figure of a Mythical Dragon (Qilin-Related Type) in Pale Jade

Pale nephrite with even russet-brown patina, scaled dragon form with curled tail, Yellow River basin

Pale Nephrite (Russet-Brown Patina) Late Zhou – Early Han, c. 300–200 BCE Mythical Dragon Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a sculptural jade figure of a mythical creature, read per the accompanying documentation as a dragon of qilin-related type, worked in the developed lapidary tradition associated with the late Zhou to early Han transition. The figure is shown in a dynamic, raised posture, with emphasis placed on the forepart of the body.

The head is massive, with pronounced brow ridges, an open mouth, and a clearly defined crest forming a vertical accent to the composition. Per the documentation, the body surface is worked with a system of small, regularly spaced scale-like facets, each individually carved, producing a rhythmic, almost textile-like surface effect. The tail is curved and closed into a sculptural scroll, linking the rear of the body into a single compositional loop; the documentation reads this motif as characteristic of jade sculpture of the Zhou–Han transitional period and associates it with ideas of continuity and cyclical renewal.

A small drilled hole at the nostril is noted in the documentation as possibly indicating use of the object in a suspended or fixed ritual context, including burial or temple practice; this page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation, not as an established function.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: nephrite (Ca₂(Mg,Fe)₅Si₈O₂₂(OH)₂), light grey with a milky-yellow undertone.
  • Colour and structure: an even russet-brown patina, described as deeply embedded in the carved micro-relief and ornamental elements.
  • Fabric: siliceous inclusions and traces of fluid-related erosion, read in the documentation as consistent with long-term burial residence; a surface showing matte recesses, soft flaking in relief zones, and natural leaching areas.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving using hard abrasives, with local drilling at the nostril and a final hand-polishing stage; no indications of modern tools or mechanical working are noted in the documentation.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — a patina deeply embedded in the carved relief, siliceous inclusions, and fluid-erosion traces — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. This page does not offer an authentication verdict; it records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the dragon (lun / qilin-related type) occupied a key role in the sacred imagery of the late Zhou and early Han tradition, read as a mediator between the celestial and terrestrial realms, a guardian of vitality and continuity, and a symbol of authority, order, and cosmic harmony.

  • the figure's role, within burial contexts, as a protective entity accompanying the soul and assisting its passage to higher realms, per the documentation's reading;
  • the vertical dynamism of the pose and the tension of the body's plasticity as an expression of an energetic, apotropaic function;
  • the closed, scroll-form tail as a compositional device read as linking the figure to ideas of continuity and cyclical renewal.

Taken together, per the documentation, the figure is read as an example of Zhou–Han transitional jade sculpture in which technical precision and a dense symbolic programme combine within a single guardian image.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
