RO-JADE-010 · Late Shang – Early Western Zhou Ritual Jade

Archaic Ritual Rhyton in Zoomorphic Jade (Gong Vessel)

Iron-rich nephrite, hybrid dragon–rhinoceros–bird form, Yellow River basin

Nephrite (Iron-Rich) Late Shang / Early Western Zhou Gong (宮) Vessel Type Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object belongs to a rare type of early Chinese ritual sculpture — a gong (宮) vessel worked in zoomorphic form. The composition combines features of several sacred animals: a dragon-like being, a rhinoceros, and a bird, consistent, per the accompanying documentation, with the hybrid iconographic system characteristic of late Shang and early Western Zhou art.

The body of the vessel is built on a principle of plastic continuity: the curved line of the body flows smoothly into a thickened nose section, recalling the beak or muzzle of a mythical creature. Spiral ear elements and the rhythmic organisation of the surface reinforce a sense of internal movement within the form. The ornamentation includes trellis-pattern (lattice) engraving, archaic zoomorphic masks, and spiral scrollwork close to early forms of the taotie tradition.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: nephrite (Ca₂(Mg,Fe)₅Si₈O₂₂(OH)₂), with pronounced iron-bearing phases.
  • Colour and structure: a light-green base transitioning into ochre-rust and brown zones; black, point-like inclusions of ferro-mineral origin are present.
  • Patina and ageing: deep, diffuse surface mineralisation; development of a microporous "sheaf-like" structure; infilling of microcracks with breakdown products of iron-bearing compounds; traces consistent with prolonged residence in a moist geochemical environment.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving of dense nephrite; sequential abrasive working with quartz and corundum materials; minimal traces of ancient drilling at structural joints; no indication of modern mechanical tooling; natural micro-fracturing characteristic of archaeological nephrite.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — diffuse mineralisation, a microporous sheaf-like secondary structure, and iron-compound infill of microcracks — is consistent with a mineralised surface layer formed over a long burial interval. The translucency detail (Fig. 3) is consistent with the zonal internal structure described in the documentation.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural assessment, not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, vessels of the gong type were used within a system of ritual libation, in both temple ceremonies and the funerary rites of high-ranking elites. The zoomorphic form is read as serving several functions simultaneously:

  • the image of a guardian spirit, protecting the ritual space;
  • a mediator between the world of the living and the world of ancestors;
  • a vessel for sacred potency, concentrated through nephrite understood as a "stone of celestial essence."

The hybridised imagery is read, per the documentation, as reflecting an early stage in the formation of Chinese cosmology, in which the animal and the supernatural were not yet held as separate categories.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date or function.
