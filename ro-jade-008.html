RO-JADE-008 · Neolithic Ritual Jade

Jade Dragon Brace (Zhulong Pendant)

Warm yellow-ochre nephrite, C-shaped form, Hongshan culture

Nephrite (Tremolite–Actinolite) Hongshan Culture Zhulong / C-Dragon Type Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object belongs to the classic type of C-shaped jade pendant associated with the Hongshan culture, known as the "dragon brace" (zhulong). The composition combines a smoothly curved ring form with a detailed head section, interpreted per the accompanying documentation as a stylised image of a mythological being combining dragon-like and pig-like totemic features. The head is worked with a high degree of plastic modelling: the eye, muzzle, and a simplified crest-like contour are indicated. The opposite end of the form tapers to a softly reduced tail element. The suspension hole is reported to have been drilled before the final polishing of the surface, consistent with the technological standards of late Neolithic ritual jade.

The surface shows a combination of a high degree of original polish and an even, natural patina formed under prolonged burial conditions.

Section 2

Working Technique

Per the accompanying documentation, the manufacturing technique includes:

  • hand carving using mineral abrasives;
  • prolonged grinding with quartz-feldspar pastes;
  • final polishing that preserves the surface micro-relief;
  • drilling of the suspension hole prior to the final finishing stage.

No traces of mechanised working, modern abrasives, or synthetic polishing were reported.

Section 3 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Scientific Examination — SEM and Mineralogical Analysis

According to the accompanying documentation, scanning electron microscopy was carried out on this object, with the following observations reported:

  • A developed layer of microcrystalline calcite (CaCO₃) with heterogeneous morphology.
  • Inclusions of iron and manganese oxides distributed within the patinated zone.
  • A microporous structure characteristic of prolonged natural ageing.
  • Silicate–carbonate infill of microcracks.

Petrographic analysis, per the accompanying documentation, points to a nephrite source in the Xiuyan deposits (Liaoning Province), traditionally associated with Hongshan ritual jade working. The reported data are stated to be consistent with natural mineralisation, without indication of artificial ageing.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination of a heterogeneous calcite layer, iron/manganese inclusions, and microporous, crack-infilled structure is consistent with a mineralised secondary surface layer formed over a long burial interval.

The SEM and petrographic observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; the underlying data have not been independently re-examined for this Atlas entry. They are presented as supporting evidence alongside the macroscopic description in Figs. 1–3, not as a standalone, independently re-verified laboratory result of this page.

Section 4

Cultural and Symbolic Context

Per the accompanying documentation, jade pendants of the "dragon brace" type held a central place in the ritual system of the Hongshan culture. Their C-shaped form is interpreted as a symbol of the cyclical movement of life energy and of the cosmological connection between heaven and earth. Such objects may have functioned as:

  • ritual attributes of altar complexes;
  • funerary symbols of status;
  • objects of cosmogonic symbolism connected with fertility and ancestor cults.

The documentation cites comparable pendants reported in major museum collections, including the National Museum of China (Beijing), the Metropolitan Museum of Art (New York), the Liaoning Provincial Museum (Shenyang), and the British Museum (London).

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. The comparative museum references have not been independently verified for this Atlas entry.

Section 5

Condition

Per the accompanying documentation, the object is in a stable archaeological condition. The surface is evenly patinated, with gently smoothed edges and natural microcracks; no traces of restoration, re-polishing, or modern intervention are reported.
