RO-JADE-021 · Late Eastern Zhou Ritual Jade

White and Russet Jade Figure of a Coiled Mythical Beast

Dense white nephrite with russet-brown patina and black organo-mineral inclusions, coiled dragon-beast form, Yellow River basin

White Nephrite (Russet Patina) Late Eastern Zhou, Warring States, c. 400–250 BCE Coiled Mythical Beast Figure Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a compact jade sculpture depicting a mythical beast, read per the accompanying documentation as tightly coiled in repose, its neck arched and limbs tucked beneath the body. Per the documentation, the catalogue reference for this object is JN-025.

Per the documentation, the head is robust, with spiralling brow forms, and the compact, rhythmically curved body is read as conveying a sense of latent energy. The figure is hand-carved with a soft polish, and the documentation notes the retention of minute tool traces described as typical of pre-imperial workmanship.

Distinctive spherical black inclusions, up to approximately 3 mm in diameter, are noted beneath the polished surface. Per the documentation, these are read as consistent with long-term capillary diffusion of iron-manganese and humic compounds within a burial environment; this page presents that reading as documentation-derived interpretation of the observed inclusions, not as an independently confirmed process.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: dense white nephrite, described as Hetian-type, showing a fibrous "sheaf-like" internal texture noted as typical of ancient tremolite jade.
  • Colour and structure: a rich russet and dark-brown patina penetrating carved recesses and natural fissures.
  • Black inclusions: spherical infiltrates up to approximately 3 mm in diameter, lying beneath the polished surface; the documentation reads their round morphology as characteristic of long-term capillary diffusion of iron-manganese and humic compounds in an anaerobic burial environment.
  • Manufacturing technology: hand carving with soft polish and minute tool traces, described in the documentation as typical of pre-imperial workmanship, without indications of modern rotary-tool or chemical treatment.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — a deeply penetrating natural patina, spherical black organo-mineral inclusions, and a fibrous internal texture — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. The documentation's characterisation of the black inclusions as "impossible to imitate" and its statement that the observed features "authenticate" the piece are the documentation's own claims; this page does not adopt them and does not offer an authentication verdict. It records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, the poised, coiled composition and stylised anatomy are read as corresponding to Warring States period aristocratic jades associated with concepts of cosmic power and spiritual guardianship. The documentation notes comparable coiled-beast examples in major collections, including holdings associated with Henan, Shanxi, and Shandong, where such figures are read as prestigious symbols of status and metaphysical protection rather than simple funerary deposits.

  • the coiled, compact composition as an expression of latent energy and restrained power, per the documentation's reading;
  • the figure's status as an aristocratic emblem of strength, protection, and noble prestige, created for high-status ritual or display;
  • the spiralling brow and robust head as part of a stylised mythical-beast vocabulary associated with Warring States ritual jade.

Taken together, per the documentation, the figure is read as an example of late Eastern Zhou aristocratic jade sculpture in which compact, coiled form and mineralogical condition combine within a single guardian image.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
