RO-JADE-024 · Late Han – Early Tang Ceremonial Jade

Small Square Jade Seal with Mythical-Beast Knob

White Hetian-type nephrite with ochre-yellow and russet-brown patina, monolithic seal with sculptural beast knob, Chang'an region, Northern China

White Hetian Nephrite Late Han – Early Tang, c. 1st c. BCE – 7th c. CE Seal with Mythical-Beast Knob Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Section 1

Object Description and Attribution

The object is a small square jade seal surmounted by a sculptural knob in the form of a mythical beast. Per the accompanying documentation, the catalogue reference for this object is WSH-12, and the piece is carved from a single block of white nephrite, combining a square base with a printing surface and a sculptural handle without any constructive separation between the two elements.

Per the documentation, the cubic base is cut with strict geometric regularity, while the beast above is treated with freer sculptural plasticity — powerful paws pressed against the seal's upper surface, a half-open mouth, and a curled tail — a contrast the documentation associates with late Han decorative style. The seal face carries a four-line inscription in zhuanshu (篆书) seal script. The documentation does not propose a specific reading of the inscription and states explicitly that a final interpretation of its content would require a separate epigraphic study based on high-quality impressions or macro-photographs; this page follows that qualification and does not offer a reading here.

Per the accompanying documentation, the beast knob is read as related to one of several mythical-guardian types — xiezhi (獬豸, associated with justice), bixie (辟邪, an evil-repelling guardian), or an early variant of tianlu (天禄, a celestial guardian); this page presents these as documentation-proposed readings rather than a settled identification.

Section 2 · Surface Transformation Analysis (STA)

Mineralogical and Technological Analysis

Per the accompanying documentation:

  • Mineral type: white Hetian-type nephrite, described as showing a tangled fibrous tremolite structure, a high degree of crystal interweaving, and characteristic translucency at thin edges.
  • Colour and patina: an ochre-yellow patina with russet-brown iron-related zones and localised calcite formations, with colour change noted along natural micro-cracks; the patina is described as penetrating carved grooves, micro-pores, and internal structural defects.
  • Manufacturing technology: sequential abrasive carving using quartz sand and corundum abrasives, tubular drilling for individual recesses, and repeated hand polishing with leather and wood polishers; the documentation notes an absence of modern diamond-tool marks.
  • Wear and ageing: multidirectional abrasive grooves, broad ancient polishing marks, gradual edge rounding, and soft smoothing of raised areas, most informative around the beast's paws.

The documentation also states that scanning electron microscopy (SEM) of "authentic jade seals of this type" typically reveals microcrystalline calcite, iron and manganese oxides, silicate soil inclusions, and natural leaching zones. This page notes explicitly that this statement concerns jade seals of this general type, not an SEM analysis carried out on this specific object; no instrumental analysis of this piece itself is documented.

Read against the Atlas's Surface Transformation Analysis (STA) framework, the reported combination — zonal patina penetrating carved grooves and structural defects, multidirectional hand-tool marks, and gradual edge rounding — is described in the documentation as consistent with long-term natural surface evolution. This is offered as a descriptive reading of the reported surface condition, not as a verdict on authenticity.

The mineralogical and technological observations above are reported as stated in the accompanying documentation; they represent a visual-structural, optical assessment — tagged Instrument Unconfirmed, as the specific magnification and device used are not documented for this object — not an independently re-verified instrumental (SEM/XRF) result of this page. The documentation's statement that the patina's distribution "is impossible to achieve by artificial colouring" is the documentation's own claim; this page does not adopt it and does not offer an authentication verdict. The documentation's closing statement attributing the object as "a genuine high-status jade seal" of the late Han–early Tang period is likewise the documentation's own conclusion, not an assessment made by this page. This page records reported surface and technical observations for reference purposes only.

Section 3

Symbolism and Function

Per the accompanying documentation, mythical guardian beasts of this kind — read alternatively as xiezhi, bixie, or an early tianlu type — carried associations in the Han period with imperial authority, the legitimacy of governance, protection of the state, and the ruler's connection to Heaven; the use of such a knob on a seal is read as consistent with an administrative-ritual function.

  • the beast knob as a marker of state authority and legitimacy, per the documentation's reading;
  • the strict cubic base as an emblem of institutional stability, in contrast with the freer plasticity of the sculptural knob;
  • the monolithic, undivided construction as evidence, per the documentation, of high technical mastery within the court seal-carving tradition.

Taken together, per the documentation, the seal is read as an example of the administrative-ritual jade seal tradition of the late Han to early Tang period, in which strict geometric form and sculptural guardian imagery combine within a single object of state or high-status use. The documentation's own comparative notes point to related seals reported in major museum collections; this page does not reproduce that comparative material, which belongs to a separate publications/bibliography resource rather than to the object record itself.

This section presents historical and ethnographic interpretation drawn from the accompanying documentation, not a verified statement about the specific intent of this object's original makers. It is offered as cultural context alongside the material evidence above, not as an independent proof of date, function, or authenticity.
